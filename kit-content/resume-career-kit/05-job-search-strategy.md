## 1. Target Company Research

**Level:** All Levels
**When to use:** Before applying anywhere, research whether a company is worth your time.

```
Help me research [COMPANY NAME] to determine if I should invest time applying there.

What I know:
- Company: [NAME]
- Industry: [INDUSTRY]
- Size: [IF KNOWN]
- Role I'd target: [JOB TITLE]

Research these dimensions and give me a recommendation:

1. **Company Health**
   - Funding history / financial stability (public filings, Crunchbase, news)
   - Growth trajectory (hiring trends — are they growing or downsizing?)
   - Recent layoffs or negative news
   - Leadership stability

2. **Employee Experience**
   - Glassdoor rating and trend (improving or declining?)
   - Common themes in employee reviews (positive and negative)
   - Average tenure (high turnover = red flag)
   - Compensation reputation (do they pay well?)

3. **Role-Specific Intelligence**
   - How often do they post this role? (High frequency = high turnover)
   - Who would I likely report to? (Research the hiring manager if identifiable)
   - Team size and structure
   - Tech stack, tools, or methodologies they use

4. **Culture Indicators**
   - Remote/hybrid/in-office policy and how employees feel about it
   - Work-life balance reputation
   - DEI reputation and actions (not just statements)
   - How they treat employees during layoffs (if applicable)

5. **Application Strategy**
   - Best way to apply (direct, referral, recruiter)
   - Do they have an employee referral program?
   - Key people to connect with on LinkedIn
   - Any known interview process details

**Final recommendation:** Based on this research, should I apply? Rate 1-10 with reasoning.
```

> **Why This Works:** Every application costs time and emotional energy. This prompt helps you invest your effort where the odds are highest and the company deserves your talent.

## 2. Dream Company List Builder

**Level:** All Levels
**When to use:** You need to build a targeted list of companies to focus your job search.

```
Help me build a targeted list of 25-30 companies to focus my job search.

My criteria:
- Target role: [JOB TITLE]
- Industry preference: [INDUSTRY/INDUSTRIES — or "open"]
- Company size preference: [STARTUP / MID-SIZE / ENTERPRISE / ANY]
- Location: [CITY/REGION / REMOTE / OPEN TO RELOCATION]
- Company stage: [PRE-SEED TO SERIES A / GROWTH STAGE / PUBLIC / ANY]
- Compensation priority: [HIGH SALARY / EQUITY UPSIDE / WORK-LIFE BALANCE / MISSION-DRIVEN / GROWTH OPPORTUNITY]
- Deal-breakers: [THINGS THAT WOULD MAKE ME SAY NO — e.g., "no fully in-office roles," "no companies under 50 people"]
- Industries I want to AVOID: [IF ANY]

My background:
- Current industry: [INDUSTRY]
- Years of experience: [NUMBER]
- Key skills: [TOP 5]
- Previous company types: [WHERE I'VE WORKED]

Build me 3 tiers:

**Tier 1 — Dream Companies (10):**
These are aspirational but realistic. Companies I'd be thrilled to work for. Higher competition, higher reward.

**Tier 2 — Strong Matches (10):**
Companies where my experience is a natural fit. Good chance of getting an interview.

**Tier 3 — Strategic Bets (5-10):**
Companies I might not have heard of but match my criteria. Less competition, hidden gems.

For each company:
- Company name
- Why it matches my criteria
- Approximate size and stage
- Where to find their job listings
- A person I should try to connect with on LinkedIn (role type, not a specific name)
- Estimated difficulty level (how competitive is getting hired there?)
```

> **Pro Tip:** Job searching is like sales — you need a pipeline. Applying to random jobs is like cold-calling from the phone book. A targeted list with tiers gives you focus and momentum.

## 3. Hidden Job Market Strategy

**Level:** Mid / Senior / Executive
**When to use:** You want to access jobs that are never publicly posted (estimated 60-80% of jobs).

```
Help me develop a strategy to access the hidden job market — positions that are filled through networking, referrals, and direct outreach before they're ever posted online.

My profile:
- Target role: [JOB TITLE]
- Industry: [INDUSTRY]
- Years of experience: [NUMBER]
- Current network strength: [STRONG / MODERATE / WEAK / STARTING FROM SCRATCH]
- Target companies: [LIST 5-10 IF YOU HAVE THEM]

Build me a hidden job market strategy:

1. **Signal Detection** — How to identify companies that are about to hire before they post:
   - Funding announcements (they'll need to staff up)
   - Executive departures (succession creates openings)
   - Product launches (new teams needed)
   - Job postings for related roles (if they're hiring a VP of Sales, they'll need sales reps soon)
   - LinkedIn activity patterns (increased recruiter activity, team growth)

2. **Direct Outreach Campaign:**
   - Who to contact at each target company (not HR — the decision maker)
   - How to find the right person (LinkedIn, company website, mutual connections)
   - Message templates for reaching out before a job is posted
   - How to frame it: "I'm exploring opportunities" vs. "I want a job" (the framing matters)

3. **Referral Mining:**
   - How to systematically identify who in my network knows someone at my target companies
   - Scripts for asking for introductions without being awkward
   - How to use LinkedIn's "People Also Viewed" and "People You May Know" features strategically

4. **Community Infiltration:**
   - Industry Slack groups, Discord servers, and communities where jobs get shared first
   - Conferences, meetups, and events (virtual and in-person) where hiring managers attend
   - Newsletters and job boards that feature roles before they hit LinkedIn

5. **Weekly Action Plan:**
   - How many outreach messages to send per week
   - How many informational interviews to schedule
   - How to track and follow up on all outreach
```

## 4. Cold LinkedIn DM Templates

**Level:** All Levels
**When to use:** You want to reach out to someone you don't know on LinkedIn — a recruiter, hiring manager, or potential connection.

```
Write LinkedIn direct message templates for cold outreach during my job search. These need to be short, specific, and get responses.

My info:
- Name: [YOUR NAME]
- Current role: [TITLE]
- Target role: [TARGET TITLE]
- Key credential: [YOUR STRONGEST RELEVANT QUALIFICATION]

Write messages for these 6 scenarios:

**1. Message to a recruiter at a target company:**
(They recruit for roles like mine)

**2. Message to a likely hiring manager:**
(I've identified who would manage this role)

**3. Message to someone in my target role at the company:**
(I want to learn about the team/culture before applying)

**4. Message to a second-degree connection:**
(We share a mutual connection I can reference)

**5. Message to someone who posted content I found valuable:**
(Building a relationship through genuine engagement)

**6. Message to a former colleague who now works at a target company:**
(Reconnecting with an ask in mind)

Rules for ALL messages:
1. Under 300 characters for connection requests, under 500 for InMail/messages
2. Personalized — reference something specific about them (their post, their background, their company)
3. Clear but not pushy ask
4. Value-first when possible (offer something, don't just ask)
5. No "I know you're busy" or "Sorry to bother you" — just be direct and respectful
6. Never attach your resume unsolicited

For each template, also provide:
- Subject line (if InMail)
- Best time to send
- Follow-up message if no response after 5 days
- Response rate expectation (set realistic expectations)
```

> **Why This Works:** Cold outreach works when it's specific, brief, and human. Most cold DMs fail because they're generic, long, or immediately ask for too much. These templates get responses because they respect the recipient's time.

## 5. Warm Introduction Requests

**Level:** All Levels
**When to use:** You want to ask someone in your network to introduce you to someone at a target company.

```
Write scripts for asking someone in my network to make an introduction to a person at my target company.

The connection chain:
- Me: [YOUR NAME, YOUR ROLE]
- My contact (the person I'll ask): [THEIR NAME, OUR RELATIONSHIP]
- Target person (who I want to meet): [THEIR NAME, THEIR ROLE AT TARGET COMPANY]
- What I want from the introduction: [INFORMATIONAL INTERVIEW / REFERRAL / GENERAL NETWORKING]

Write messages for these scenarios:

**1. Asking a close friend/former colleague:**
(I know them well, can be casual)

**2. Asking a professional acquaintance:**
(We know each other but aren't close)

**3. Asking someone I haven't spoken to in over a year:**
(Need to reconnect first)

**4. Asking after they've already agreed to help:**
(A "forwardable" blurb they can send to the target person)

For each:
1. Make it easy for them to say yes (low effort on their part)
2. Include a "forwardable" blurb they can copy-paste when making the intro (they don't want to write it themselves)
3. Give them an easy out if they're not comfortable
4. Be specific about what I'm asking (not just "can you connect us" — WHY)
5. Offer to reciprocate
6. Keep it under 150 words

Also provide:
- The "double opt-in" introduction format (the gold standard for professional intros)
- How to follow up if they say yes but don't follow through
- How to thank them after the introduction is made
```

## 6. Informational Interview Scripts

**Level:** All Levels
**When to use:** You want to have a learning conversation with someone in your target role, company, or industry.

```
Help me prepare for an informational interview with someone at [COMPANY NAME] who works as a [THEIR ROLE].

Context:
- How I got this meeting: [REFERRAL / COLD OUTREACH / ALUMNI CONNECTION / ETC.]
- My target role: [JOB TITLE]
- What I most want to learn: [YOUR TOP 3 QUESTIONS]
- My experience level: [ENTRY / MID / SENIOR]

Write complete scripts for:

**1. The outreach message asking for the meeting:**
- Keep it under 100 words
- Ask for 15-20 minutes (not 30 — lower commitment)
- Make it clear this is NOT a job ask (it's a learning conversation)
- Suggest a specific day/time

**2. The interview itself — questions to ask (in order):**

Opening (warm up, 2 minutes):
- [2 rapport-building questions about their background]

Career insights (core, 10 minutes):
- [5 questions about their career path, role, and daily work]

Company intelligence (valuable, 5 minutes):
- [3 questions about the company culture, team, and growth]

Advice (closing, 3 minutes):
- [2 questions asking for their advice for someone in my position]

**3. The transition to opportunity (only if appropriate):**
- How to mention I'm job searching WITHOUT making this feel like a bait-and-switch
- The exact sentence to use
- When NOT to bring it up

**4. The follow-up thank-you message:**
- Sent within 24 hours
- References something specific from the conversation
- Under 100 words

**5. The long-term relationship maintainer:**
- How to stay in touch without being annoying
- What to send them 1 month, 3 months, and 6 months later
```

> **Pro Tip:** Informational interviews are the single most underused job search tactic. They give you insider knowledge, build your network, and often lead directly to referrals — but only if you approach them as genuine learning conversations, not stealth job interviews.

## 8. Application Tracker System

**Level:** All Levels
**When to use:** You need a system to track all your job applications without losing your mind.

```
Help me design a job application tracking system I can maintain in [SPREADSHEET / NOTION / AIRTABLE / SIMPLE DOC].

My job search scope:
- Number of applications per week: [ESTIMATED NUMBER]
- Number of target companies: [NUMBER]
- How organized I currently am: [NOT AT ALL / SOMEWHAT / VERY]

Design a tracker with these fields:

**Core Fields:**
1. Company name
2. Role title
3. Job posting URL
4. Date applied
5. Application method (direct, referral, recruiter)
6. Status (Applied → Phone Screen → Interview 1 → Interview 2 → Final → Offer → Rejected)
7. Next action and date
8. Contact person (name, email, LinkedIn)
9. Salary range (posted or estimated)
10. Notes

**Advanced Fields:**
11. Referral contact (who referred me, if applicable)
12. Resume version used
13. Cover letter version used
14. Follow-up dates (when I followed up and response)
15. Interview dates and interviewers
16. Feedback received
17. Red flags noted
18. Excitement level (1-5)

**Dashboards/Views I need:**
- "Needs Action" — applications where the ball is in my court
- "Waiting" — applications where I'm waiting to hear back
- "This Week's Interviews" — upcoming interview prep
- "Follow-Up Due" — applications where it's time to check in
- "Dead" — rejections and withdrawn applications (keep for learning)

Also provide:
- A weekly review checklist (what to update every Friday)
- Status definitions (when does "Applied" become "Phone Screen"?)
- When to archive/remove old applications
- How to use this data to improve my search (patterns, conversion rates)
```

> **Why This Works:** Job seekers who track their applications methodically are more effective because they never miss a follow-up, they learn from patterns, and they stay organized under the emotional stress of job searching.

## 9. Job Description Decoder

**Level:** All Levels
**When to use:** You want to understand what a job description REALLY means — the subtext, the priorities, and the red flags.

```
Decode this job description for me. Tell me what they actually mean, what they really need, and whether I should apply.

Job Description:
[PASTE THE FULL JOB DESCRIPTION]

My background:
- Years of experience: [NUMBER]
- Key skills: [LIST TOP 5-7]
- Industry: [INDUSTRY]

Decode:

1. **Translation** — For each requirement or bullet point, tell me:
   - What they wrote
   - What they actually mean
   - How important it really is (must-have vs. nice-to-have vs. wishlist)

2. **Red Flag Analysis:**
   - Are there any red flags in this description? (unrealistic expectations, "wear many hats" = under-resourced, "fast-paced" = chaotic, "competitive salary" = they might lowball)
   - What's NOT in the description that should be? (missing salary, vague responsibilities)

3. **Priority Ranking:**
   - What are the top 3 things they ACTUALLY care about? (Not what's listed first — what they'd filter for)
   - What keywords will the ATS scan for?

4. **My Fit Assessment:**
   - Match percentage (how well do I fit this role?)
   - Strengths (where I exceed requirements)
   - Gaps (where I fall short)
   - Is this a realistic application or a reach?

5. **Application Strategy:**
   - If I apply, what 3 things should I emphasize in my resume/cover letter?
   - Should I address any gaps proactively?
   - Is this worth tailoring my resume for, or is it a long shot?

6. **Salary Estimate:**
   - Based on the title, company, location, and requirements, estimate the salary range
```

## 10. Personal Brand Statement

**Level:** All Levels
**When to use:** You need a clear, consistent message about who you are and what you offer — used across LinkedIn, networking, cover letters, and interviews.

```
Help me craft a personal brand statement that I can use consistently across my job search — LinkedIn, networking events, cover letters, and interviews.

About me:
- My profession: [WHAT I DO]
- My specialization: [MY NICHE]
- Years of experience: [NUMBER]
- The problem I solve: [WHAT I DO FOR COMPANIES/TEAMS]
- My unique approach: [WHAT'S DIFFERENT ABOUT HOW I DO IT]
- My biggest proof point: [STRONGEST ACHIEVEMENT WITH METRICS]
- My professional values: [WHAT I STAND FOR]
- My target audience: [WHO I WANT TO WORK FOR]

Create:

1. **One-liner** (10 words or less):
   - Use this in LinkedIn headline, email signature, name tags

2. **Elevator pitch** (30 seconds / 75 words):
   - Use this when someone asks "What do you do?" at networking events

3. **Brand paragraph** (60 seconds / 150 words):
   - Use this in LinkedIn About section, cover letter opening, or interview "Tell me about yourself"

4. **Brand keywords** (10 words):
   - Words that should appear consistently across all my materials

5. **Brand story** (2 minutes / 300 words):
   - The narrative version — how I got here, what drives me, where I'm going
   - Use this for longer networking conversations or podcast/interview appearances

All versions should be:
- Consistent in message (same core idea, different lengths)
- Specific to me (not interchangeable with someone else in my field)
- Focused on value I provide, not just what I do
- Memorable — includes a hook or unique angle
```

## 11. Elevator Pitch Writer

**Level:** All Levels
**When to use:** You need a polished 30-second introduction for networking, career fairs, or when someone asks "What do you do?"

```
Write a 30-second elevator pitch for my job search that's natural, memorable, and opens doors.

About me:
- Name: [YOUR NAME]
- What I do: [YOUR PROFESSION]
- Specialization: [YOUR NICHE]
- Biggest relevant win: [ACHIEVEMENT WITH METRICS]
- What I'm looking for: [TARGET ROLE/COMPANY TYPE]
- My personality in one word: [e.g., "strategic," "creative," "analytical"]

The setting: [NETWORKING EVENT / CAREER FAIR / CASUAL INTRODUCTION / FORMAL INTERVIEW]

Write 3 versions:

**Version 1 — The Results-Led Pitch:**
"I'm [name], and I [what you do]. Most recently, I [achievement]. Now I'm looking for [target]."

**Version 2 — The Problem-Solver Pitch:**
"You know how [common problem in your field]? I [how you solve it]. For example, I [achievement]."

**Version 3 — The Story Pitch:**
"I got into [your field] because [brief origin story]. That's led me to [current expertise], where I [achievement]."

For each version:
1. Keep it under 75 words (30 seconds when spoken naturally)
2. Include one specific number or metric
3. End with an opening for the other person to respond ("What about you?" or "I'd love to hear about what your team is working on")
4. Must sound natural when spoken aloud — not robotic or rehearsed
5. Test: would this be interesting at a party, not just at a career fair?
```

## 13. Career Narrative Builder

**Level:** Mid / Senior / Executive
**When to use:** Your career path looks scattered and you need a cohesive story that makes sense.

```
Help me build a coherent career narrative that connects all my experiences into a compelling story.

My career path:
1. [ROLE 1]: [TITLE at COMPANY, DATES, BRIEF DESCRIPTION]
2. [ROLE 2]: [TITLE at COMPANY, DATES, BRIEF DESCRIPTION]
3. [ROLE 3]: [TITLE at COMPANY, DATES, BRIEF DESCRIPTION]
4. [ROLE 4]: [TITLE at COMPANY, DATES, BRIEF DESCRIPTION]
5. [CURRENT/MOST RECENT]: [TITLE at COMPANY, DATES, BRIEF DESCRIPTION]

This path might look scattered because: [WHY IT LOOKS RANDOM — industry changes, pivots, gaps, lateral moves, etc.]

Target role: [WHAT I WANT NEXT]

Help me:

1. **Find the thread** — What's the common theme or skill that runs through all these roles? (Even if I can't see it, it's there.)

2. **Build the narrative** — Write a 2-minute story of my career that makes each transition feel intentional and progressive. Structure: "I started in X, where I learned Y. That led me to Z, where I..." Each role should build on the previous one.

3. **Handle the "Why did you leave?" question** — For each transition, give me a one-sentence explanation that frames it positively.

4. **Position the next move** — Make my target role feel like the logical, inevitable next step in my story.

5. **Identify the punchline** — What's the ONE sentence that summarizes my entire career story? (This becomes my brand.)

The narrative should work in:
- Interviews ("Tell me about yourself")
- Cover letters (opening paragraph)
- LinkedIn About section
- Networking conversations
```

> **Why This Works:** The biggest career storytelling mistake is treating each role as a separate chapter. The best narratives find the thread — the skill, passion, or mission — that runs through everything. Even if your path was chaotic, the story doesn't have to be.
