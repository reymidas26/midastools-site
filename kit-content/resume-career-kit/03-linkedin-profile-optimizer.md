## 1. Headline Formula Generator

**Level:** All Levels
**When to use:** Your headline is your most visible real estate on LinkedIn. It appears in search results, comments, connection requests, and messages.

```
Generate 10 LinkedIn headline options for my profile. The headline should make recruiters click on my profile when it appears in search results.

About me:
- Current or target title: [JOB TITLE]
- Specialization: [YOUR NICHE OR EXPERTISE]
- Industry: [INDUSTRY]
- Years of experience: [NUMBER]
- Key differentiator: [WHAT MAKES YOU DIFFERENT FROM OTHERS WITH THE SAME TITLE]
- Target roles: [WHAT I WANT TO BE FOUND FOR]

Generate 10 headlines using these proven formulas:
1. Title + Specialization + Key Result (e.g., "Senior Product Manager | B2B SaaS | Grew ARR from $2M to $15M")
2. Value Proposition headline (what you DO for companies, not what you ARE)
3. Keyword-stacked headline (for maximum search visibility)
4. Authority headline (includes social proof — publications, companies, awards)
5. Mission-driven headline (for purpose-oriented roles)
6. Niche expert headline (hyper-specific)
7. Results-driven headline (leads with a metric)
8. Industry + function combination (for career changers)
9. The "I help [WHO] do [WHAT]" formula
10. Creative/memorable headline (for creative industries only)

For each headline:
- Keep it under 120 characters (LinkedIn's limit)
- Front-load the most important keywords (search results truncate after ~60 characters)
- Explain when/why to use this specific version
```

> **Why This Works:** Your headline is the #1 factor in whether a recruiter clicks your profile. It appears everywhere — search results, comments, messages, People You May Know. Most people waste it with just their job title.

> **Pro Tip:** Use the pipe symbol (|) or bullet (·) to separate concepts. Avoid emojis in headlines — they look unprofessional to most recruiters and take up character space.

## 2. About Section Writer

**Level:** All Levels
**When to use:** Your About section is blank, generic, or reads like a resume summary.

```
Write a compelling LinkedIn About section that makes recruiters want to reach out and makes connections want to engage.

About me:
- Current role: [JOB TITLE at COMPANY]
- Industry: [INDUSTRY]
- Years of experience: [NUMBER]
- Core expertise areas: [LIST 3-5 AREAS]
- Career journey in brief: [2-3 SENTENCES ABOUT YOUR PATH]
- Biggest professional achievement: [YOUR TOP WIN WITH METRICS]
- What I'm known for among colleagues: [YOUR REPUTATION / WHAT PEOPLE COME TO YOU FOR]
- Professional passion or mission: [WHAT DRIVES YOU]
- What I'm looking for (if job searching): [TARGET ROLE/INDUSTRY — or leave blank if not actively searching]
- Personal touch: [A HOBBY, INTEREST, OR FACT THAT MAKES YOU HUMAN]

Write 2 versions of my About section:

VERSION A — Professional & Results-Focused:
1. Open with a hook (first 3 lines are visible before "see more" — they must earn the click)
2. Tell my career story in a way that shows progression and purpose
3. Include 2-3 key achievements with metrics
4. List my core areas of expertise
5. End with what I'm looking for or a call to action
6. Use first person ("I" not "they")
7. Keep it under 2,600 characters (LinkedIn's limit)
8. Use line breaks and white space — no giant blocks of text

VERSION B — Conversational & Story-Driven:
1. Open with a story, question, or bold statement
2. Be more personal and voice-forward
3. Same core content but told in a more engaging, approachable way
4. Better for creative industries or people who want to stand out

For both versions, the first 3 lines must work as a standalone hook — most people never click "see more."
```

> **Pro Tip:** The first 3 lines of your About section are everything. LinkedIn truncates after approximately 300 characters. Your hook must be strong enough to earn the click. Never start with "I am a [title] with [X] years of experience..." — everyone does that.

## 3. Experience Section Optimizer

**Level:** All Levels
**When to use:** Your experience section is just your resume pasted into LinkedIn (or worse, blank).

```
Rewrite my LinkedIn experience entries to be optimized for LinkedIn specifically (not just a copy of my resume).

My experience:

Role 1:
- Title: [JOB TITLE]
- Company: [COMPANY NAME]
- Duration: [START DATE — END DATE]
- What I did (rough notes): [DESCRIBE YOUR WORK AND ACHIEVEMENTS]

Role 2:
- Title: [JOB TITLE]
- Company: [COMPANY NAME]
- Duration: [START DATE — END DATE]
- What I did: [DESCRIBE YOUR WORK AND ACHIEVEMENTS]

Role 3:
- Title: [JOB TITLE]
- Company: [COMPANY NAME]
- Duration: [START DATE — END DATE]
- What I did: [DESCRIBE YOUR WORK AND ACHIEVEMENTS]

For each role, write a LinkedIn experience entry that:
1. Starts with a 2-3 sentence paragraph describing the role scope and context (unlike a resume, LinkedIn allows narrative)
2. Follows with 3-5 bullet points focused on achievements with metrics
3. Includes relevant keywords that recruiters search for in my industry
4. Is more conversational than a resume (LinkedIn is a social platform, not a document)
5. Tags relevant skills to each role (suggest which LinkedIn skills to associate)
6. Mentions notable projects, clients, or initiatives where appropriate

Key difference from resume: LinkedIn lets you tell more of the story. Use that space. Recruiters on LinkedIn expect more context than a resume provides.
```

> **Why This Works:** Most people either leave their experience section blank or dump their resume into it. LinkedIn experience should be richer than a resume — you have more space, you can tell stories, and recruiters expect more context.

## 4. Featured Section Strategy

**Level:** Mid / Senior / Executive
**When to use:** Your Featured section is empty, and you're missing an opportunity to showcase your best work.

```
Help me curate my LinkedIn Featured section to make the strongest possible impression on profile visitors.

What I have to work with:
- Articles I've written: [LIST ANY ARTICLES, BLOG POSTS, OR PUBLICATIONS]
- Presentations or talks: [LIST ANY SPEAKING ENGAGEMENTS, SLIDESHARES, ETC.]
- Projects with public URLs: [LIST WEBSITES, APPS, PORTFOLIO ITEMS]
- Media coverage: [ANY ARTICLES MENTIONING YOU OR YOUR WORK]
- Notable LinkedIn posts: [YOUR MOST ENGAGED POSTS, IF ANY]
- Awards or certifications: [ANYTHING WITH A URL OR IMAGE]
- Documents or PDFs: [CASE STUDIES, WHITE PAPERS, REPORTS]

My target audience: [RECRUITERS IN X FIELD / POTENTIAL CLIENTS / INDUSTRY PEERS]
What I want visitors to think: [e.g., "This person is an expert in..." or "I should hire this person because..."]

Help me:
1. Select the 3-5 strongest items for my Featured section (quality over quantity)
2. Determine the best order (what should be first — it gets the most visibility)
3. Write a compelling title and description for each featured item (LinkedIn shows a brief caption)
4. Suggest items I should create if I don't have enough (a quick article, a post, a document)
5. Recommend the ideal mix (e.g., 1 article + 1 project + 1 media mention + 1 document)

If I don't have much to feature yet, give me 3 quick-win ideas for content I can create this week that would be worth featuring.
```

## 5. Skills Section Curator

**Level:** All Levels
**When to use:** Your skills section is a random mess of 50 skills or completely empty.

```
Help me optimize my LinkedIn Skills section for maximum recruiter visibility.

My target role: [JOB TITLE]
My industry: [INDUSTRY]
My experience level: [ENTRY / MID / SENIOR / EXECUTIVE]

My current skills list:
[LIST ALL YOUR CURRENT LINKEDIN SKILLS — OR SAY "STARTING FROM SCRATCH"]

Target job descriptions (paste 2-3 job postings for roles you want):
[PASTE EXCERPTS FROM JOB DESCRIPTIONS, ESPECIALLY THE REQUIREMENTS/SKILLS SECTIONS]

Help me:
1. Identify the top 50 skills I should have listed (LinkedIn's max)
2. Rank them so my top 3 "pinned" skills are the most impactful (these show on your profile preview)
3. Remove any skills that are too generic ("Communication"), too basic ("Microsoft Office"), or potentially harmful
4. Add skills I'm missing that appear frequently in my target job descriptions
5. Organize them into mental categories: must-have, nice-to-have, differentiators
6. Identify which skills I should focus on getting endorsements for (and from whom)

Important: LinkedIn's search algorithm weighs skills heavily. The right skills section can be the difference between appearing on page 1 or page 10 of recruiter searches.
```

> **Pro Tip:** Your top 3 pinned skills should be the exact terms recruiters search for. Not "Marketing" — but "Digital Marketing Strategy" or "B2B SaaS Marketing." Specificity wins in LinkedIn search.

## 6. Recommendation Request Writer

**Level:** All Levels
**When to use:** You want to ask someone for a LinkedIn recommendation but don't know how to ask without being awkward.

```
Write a recommendation request message I can send to a colleague or manager. I want to ask for a LinkedIn recommendation without being awkward or presumptuous.

The person I'm asking:
- Name: [THEIR NAME]
- Our relationship: [MANAGER / COLLEAGUE / CLIENT / MENTOR / DIRECT REPORT]
- How long we worked together: [DURATION]
- What they saw me do well: [SPECIFIC PROJECTS OR SKILLS THEY COULD SPEAK TO]
- Our current relationship status: [STILL IN TOUCH / HAVEN'T SPOKEN IN A WHILE]

My situation:
- Am I job searching? [YES/NO — this affects the tone]
- Target role: [JOB TITLE, if job searching]
- What I'd love them to highlight: [SPECIFIC SKILLS, ACHIEVEMENTS, OR QUALITIES]

Write 2 versions:

VERSION A — Casual (for someone I'm still in touch with):
- Warm, personal, easy for them to say yes
- Under 100 words

VERSION B — Reconnecting (for someone I haven't talked to in a while):
- Starts by reconnecting before making the ask
- Acknowledges the time gap
- Under 150 words

For both versions:
1. Make the ask specific — tell them what I'd love them to mention (this makes it easier for them to write)
2. Give them an easy out — no pressure
3. Offer to write one for them in return
4. Don't be overly formal or groveling
```

> **Why This Works:** Most people don't ask for recommendations because they don't know how. The secret is making it easy: tell them exactly what to write about, and they'll say yes and write something great.

## 7. Recommendation Writer (For Others)

**Level:** All Levels
**When to use:** Someone asked you for a recommendation, or you want to write one proactively (which often triggers a reciprocal recommendation).

```

About the person:
- Their name: [NAME]
- Their role when we worked together: [THEIR TITLE]
- Our working relationship: [THEY REPORTED TO ME / I REPORTED TO THEM / PEERS / CLIENT-VENDOR / ETC.]
- How long we worked together: [DURATION]
- The project or context: [WHAT WE WORKED ON TOGETHER]

What I want to highlight:
- Their #1 strength: [SPECIFIC SKILL OR QUALITY]
- A specific example of them being excellent: [A REAL STORY OR MOMENT]
- The impact they had: [WHAT CHANGED BECAUSE OF THEIR WORK]
- Who would benefit from hiring them: [TYPE OF TEAM OR COMPANY]

Write a LinkedIn recommendation that:
1. Opens with a strong, specific statement (not "I had the pleasure of working with...")
2. Includes one concrete story or example (specificity = credibility)
3. Highlights 2-3 qualities with evidence
4. Ends with a clear endorsement ("I'd work with [Name] again in a heartbeat" or similar)

6. Is 100-200 words (short enough to read, long enough to be meaningful)
```

## 8. Job Search Post Creator

**Level:** All Levels
**When to use:** You're publicly announcing you're looking for work on LinkedIn.

```
Write a LinkedIn post announcing that I'm looking for a new role. I want it to be effective without being desperate or oversharing.

My situation:
- Current status: [LAID OFF / LEFT VOLUNTARILY / CONTRACT ENDED / SEEKING WHILE EMPLOYED / NEW GRAD]
- What happened (if comfortable sharing): [BRIEF CONTEXT — OR "I'd rather not explain publicly"]
- Target role: [JOB TITLE AND INDUSTRY]
- Location preference: [REMOTE / CITY / OPEN TO RELOCATION]
- Key strengths: [TOP 3 SKILLS OR EXPERIENCES]
- Biggest career achievement: [YOUR MOST IMPRESSIVE WIN]
- What kind of company: [STARTUP / ENTERPRISE / SPECIFIC INDUSTRY / ETC.]

Write 2 versions:

VERSION A — "Open to Work" Announcement:
- Professional, optimistic, specific about what I'm looking for
- Includes what I bring (not just what I want)
- Has a clear call to action (DM me, share this post, tag someone)
- Ends with relevant hashtags

VERSION B — Soft Search (Not Publicly Announcing):
- A thought leadership post that subtly signals I'm open to opportunities
- Shares an insight from my field
- Positions my expertise
- Includes a low-key "I'm exploring what's next" mention

For both:
1. Never sound desperate or bitter
2. Don't badmouth a previous employer
3. Be specific about what I'm looking for (vague = unhelpful)
4. Make it shareable — give people a reason to repost
5. Keep it under 1,300 characters for optimal reach
```

> **Pro Tip:** "Open to Work" posts that include specific skills and target roles get 2x more engagement than vague ones. "Looking for marketing roles" is weak. "Looking for Head of Growth roles at B2B SaaS companies, specializing in PLG and demand gen" is strong.

## 10. Connection Request Templates

**Level:** All Levels
**When to use:** You want to expand your network strategically during a job search.

```
Write 5 LinkedIn connection request messages for different scenarios. Each must fit within LinkedIn's 300-character limit for connection notes.

My info:
- Name: [YOUR NAME]
- Current role: [TITLE]
- Target role/industry: [WHAT I'M LOOKING FOR]
- Current company or school: [FOR CONTEXT]

Write connection requests for these scenarios:

1. **Recruiter at a target company** — I want to connect with a recruiter who works at a company I'm interested in
2. **Hiring manager for a specific role** — I found the likely hiring manager for a role I'm applying to
3. **Alumni from my school** — Someone who went to the same university and works in my target industry
4. **Speaker or thought leader I admire** — I attended their talk or read their content
5. **Former colleague reconnection** — Someone I worked with years ago who might have leads

For each:
1. Personalize it — reference something specific (their content, their company, our connection)
2. State why I want to connect (be honest, not vague)
3. Keep it under 300 characters (LinkedIn's limit)
4. Don't ask for a job directly — build the relationship first
5. End with a low-friction ask or statement

Also provide:
- 3 connection request messages to AVOID (common mistakes people make)
- Tips for when to send requests (best days/times)
```

> **Why This Works:** A personalized connection request has a 5x higher acceptance rate than a blank one. These templates give you a structure while still sounding personal.
