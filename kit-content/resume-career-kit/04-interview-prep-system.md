## 1. Company Deep-Dive Research

**Level:** All Levels
**When to use:** Before any interview. Start here.

```
Help me do deep research on [COMPANY NAME] to prepare for an interview for a [JOB TITLE] position.

What I already know:
- Company website: [URL]
- Industry: [INDUSTRY]
- Size: [APPROXIMATE EMPLOYEES AND/OR REVENUE]
- Stage: [STARTUP / GROWTH / ENTERPRISE / PUBLIC]

Research and summarize:

1. **Company Overview**
   - What they do (in one sentence a 10-year-old could understand)
   - Business model (how they make money)
   - Target customers
   - Key products or services

2. **Recent News & Developments** (last 6-12 months)
   - Funding rounds, acquisitions, or financial results
   - Product launches or major updates
   - Leadership changes
   - Press coverage or controversies

3. **Competitive Landscape**
   - Top 3-5 competitors
   - How [COMPANY] differentiates itself
   - Market position (leader, challenger, niche player?)

4. **Culture & Values**
   - Stated company values
   - Glassdoor/employee sentiment themes
   - Work style (remote, hybrid, in-office)

5. **Challenges & Opportunities**
   - What business challenges are they likely facing?
   - What opportunities could they be pursuing?
   - How does my role ([JOB TITLE]) connect to those challenges?

6. **Interview Intelligence**
   - Common interview questions at this company (from Glassdoor/forums)
   - Interview format and process (how many rounds, who interviews)
   - Red flags to watch for

7. **Talking Points for Me**
   - 3 things I can reference in the interview to show I've done my homework
   - 2 smart questions I can ask based on this research
   - 1 insight about their business I can share to stand out
```

> **Why This Works:** 47% of interviewers say they'd reject a candidate who had little knowledge of the company. This prompt turns you into the most prepared person in the room.

## 2. STAR Method Answer Builder

**Level:** All Levels
**When to use:** For preparing answers to any behavioral ("Tell me about a time when...") interview question.

```
Help me build STAR method answers for behavioral interview questions. I'll give you my rough experiences, and you'll structure them into polished STAR responses.

Target role: [JOB TITLE] at [COMPANY NAME]

Here are my experiences (raw, unstructured):

EXPERIENCE 1: [DESCRIBE A WORK SITUATION — what happened, what you did, what resulted. Be specific even if messy.]

EXPERIENCE 2: [DESCRIBE ANOTHER WORK SITUATION]

EXPERIENCE 3: [DESCRIBE ANOTHER WORK SITUATION]

EXPERIENCE 4: [DESCRIBE ANOTHER WORK SITUATION]

EXPERIENCE 5: [DESCRIBE ANOTHER WORK SITUATION]

For each experience, create a polished STAR answer:

**S — Situation:** Set the scene in 2-3 sentences. Include context (company, team, stakes).
**T — Task:** What was your specific responsibility or challenge? 1-2 sentences.
**A — Action:** What exactly did YOU do? (Not the team — YOU.) This should be the longest section. 3-5 sentences with specific actions.
**R — Result:** What happened? Include metrics wherever possible. 2-3 sentences.

Then tell me:
- Which common interview questions each story best answers
- How to adapt each story for different questions (one story can answer multiple questions)
- How long each answer should take to deliver verbally (aim for 90-120 seconds)
- A one-sentence "teaser" version of each story I can use if the answer needs to be shorter

Also flag: Which important behavioral themes am I missing stories for? (e.g., conflict resolution, failure, leadership, innovation, working under pressure)
```

> **Pro Tip:** Prepare 8-10 STAR stories that can be adapted to answer almost any behavioral question. You don't need a unique story for every possible question — you need versatile stories you can angle differently.

## 3. Tell Me About Yourself Script

**Level:** All Levels
**When to use:** This is almost always the first question. Nail it and the rest of the interview flows easier.

```
Write a "Tell me about yourself" answer for my interview at [COMPANY NAME] for a [JOB TITLE] position.

My background:
- Current role: [TITLE at COMPANY]
- Years of experience: [NUMBER]
- Career path summary: [BRIEF JOURNEY — e.g., "Started in consulting, moved to product management at tech companies"]
- Biggest relevant achievement: [YOUR TOP WIN WITH METRICS]
- Why I'm interested in this specific role: [YOUR GENUINE REASON]
- What I bring that others might not: [YOUR DIFFERENTIATOR]

Write 3 versions:

VERSION A — The Chronological (60 seconds):
- Brief past → present → future arc
- "I started in [X], developed expertise in [Y], and now I'm excited about [Z]"

VERSION B — The Achievement-Led (60 seconds):
- Open with your most impressive result
- Then explain the journey that got you there

VERSION C — The Mission-Driven (60 seconds):
- Open with WHY you do what you do
- Connect your purpose to this role and company

For all versions:
1. Keep it under 90 seconds when spoken aloud (approximately 200-250 words)
2. End by connecting to THIS role at THIS company
3. Include one specific metric or achievement
4. Make it conversational — not a rehearsed speech
5. Avoid reciting your resume chronologically (they can read that)
6. Leave hooks that invite follow-up questions about your strengths
```

> **Why This Works:** "Tell me about yourself" is not an invitation to recite your resume. It's your chance to frame the narrative. Control the story here, and you control the rest of the interview.

## 4. Why Do You Want This Job? Script

**Level:** All Levels
**When to use:** When they ask why you want this specific role, company, or industry.

```
Write a compelling answer to "Why do you want this job?" for my interview at [COMPANY NAME] for [JOB TITLE].

My honest reasons (I'll tell you the truth, you help me say it professionally):
- Why this company: [YOUR REAL REASON — be honest, even if it's partly about money, location, prestige, etc.]
- Why this role: [YOUR REAL REASON]
- Why now: [WHY THIS MAKES SENSE AT THIS POINT IN YOUR CAREER]
- What specifically excites me about the job description: [SPECIFIC ASPECTS]
- What I know about the company: [RESEARCH YOU'VE DONE]
- How this fits my career goals: [WHERE YOU SEE THIS LEADING]

Write an answer that:
1. Addresses three layers: why this COMPANY, why this ROLE, and why NOW
2. References something specific about the company (not generic flattery)
3. Connects my skills and experience to the role's requirements
4. Shows genuine enthusiasm without being over-the-top
5. Is under 90 seconds when spoken
6. Handles potential awkward truths (if I'm leaving a bad situation, if salary is a factor, etc.) with grace
7. Ends with forward-looking energy

Also write a follow-up answer for: "Why are you interested in our company specifically?" (this is a slightly different question that needs a more company-focused answer)
```

## 5. Weakness Question Handler

**Level:** All Levels
**When to use:** For the dreaded "What's your biggest weakness?" question.

```
Help me prepare an answer to "What's your biggest weakness?" that's honest, strategic, and doesn't use a disguised strength.

My actual areas for improvement (be honest with me, I'll be honest with you):
1. [REAL WEAKNESS — e.g., "I struggle with public speaking," "I take too long on details," "I avoid confrontation"]
2. [ANOTHER REAL WEAKNESS]
3. [ANOTHER REAL WEAKNESS]

The role I'm interviewing for: [JOB TITLE]
Key requirements of the role: [TOP 3-4 REQUIREMENTS]

For each weakness, help me:
1. Evaluate if it's safe to share for THIS role (don't reveal a weakness that's a core job requirement)
2. Write an answer using this structure:
   - Name the weakness honestly (1 sentence)
   - Give a brief, specific example of how it's shown up (1-2 sentences)
   - Describe specific steps you've taken to improve (2-3 sentences)
   - Share progress or a recent win that shows improvement (1 sentence)
3. Keep the total answer under 60 seconds

RULES:
- No "I'm a perfectionist" or "I work too hard" — interviewers see right through this
- No weakness that would disqualify me for the role
- The improvement steps must be specific and real (not "I'm working on it")
- Show self-awareness and growth, not just a weakness

Recommend which of my three weaknesses is the safest and most compelling to share for THIS specific role.
```

> **Pro Tip:** The weakness question isn't about the weakness — it's about self-awareness and growth mindset. Interviewers want to see that you can identify, own, and improve your weak spots.

## 7. Technical Interview Prep

**Level:** Mid / Senior
**When to use:** You have a technical interview round (coding, system design, domain expertise, etc.).

```
Help me prepare for a technical interview for [JOB TITLE] at [COMPANY NAME].

Technical focus areas from the job description:
[LIST THE TECHNICAL REQUIREMENTS OR SKILLS MENTIONED]

My technical background:
- Strongest technical areas: [LIST]
- Areas I'm less confident in: [LIST]
- Years of technical experience: [NUMBER]
- Most complex technical project: [BRIEF DESCRIPTION]

Interview format (if known): [CODING CHALLENGE / SYSTEM DESIGN / WHITEBOARD / TAKE-HOME / LIVE DEBUGGING / DOMAIN EXPERTISE Q&A]

Help me prepare:

1. **Likely technical questions** — Generate 15 technical questions they might ask based on the job description and company. Range from fundamentals to advanced.

2. **Concept refresher** — For each major technical area in the job description, give me a concise refresher (key concepts, common pitfalls, best practices). Keep each to 3-5 bullet points.

3. **Problem-solving framework** — Give me a step-by-step approach for tackling technical questions live:
   - How to clarify the problem
   - How to think out loud
   - How to handle getting stuck
   - How to discuss tradeoffs

4. **Red flag answers** — What technical answers or behaviors would immediately concern an interviewer? What should I avoid saying or doing?

5. **Questions I should ask** — 3 technical questions I can ask that demonstrate deep knowledge

6. **Practice plan** — A 3-day prep plan to review the most important technical concepts
```

## 8. Situational Question Prep

**Level:** Mid / Senior / Executive
**When to use:** Preparing for "What would you do if..." hypothetical questions.

```
Help me prepare for situational interview questions (hypothetical scenarios) for a [JOB TITLE] role.

My industry: [INDUSTRY]
My level: [MID / SENIOR / EXECUTIVE]
The company: [COMPANY NAME AND TYPE]

Generate 10 situational questions I'm likely to face, and for each one:

1. The question itself
2. What the interviewer is evaluating
3. A structured framework for answering (not a scripted answer — a framework I can apply)
4. Key elements my answer MUST include
5. A sample answer outline specific to my role
6. Common mistakes candidates make on this question

Cover these scenario types:
- A project going off the rails
- Disagreeing with your boss
- Making a decision with incomplete information
- Prioritizing between competing urgent tasks
- Dealing with an underperforming team member
- Responding to a customer crisis
- Managing a tight deadline with insufficient resources
- Navigating a cross-functional conflict
- Adapting to a sudden change in direction
- Ethical dilemma or integrity test

For situational questions, use this framework:
1. Acknowledge the complexity
2. Share your approach/framework
3. Walk through your decision process
4. State the action you'd take
5. Anticipate the follow-up question
```

> **Why This Works:** Situational questions test your judgment and decision-making process, not your storytelling. The interviewer wants to see HOW you think, not just what you've done.

## 9. Questions to Ask the Interviewer

**Level:** All Levels
**When to use:** When they ask "Do you have any questions for us?" (always say yes).

```
Generate smart, memorable questions I can ask during my interview for [JOB TITLE] at [COMPANY NAME].

Interview context:
- Interview round: [PHONE SCREEN / FIRST ROUND / SECOND ROUND / FINAL ROUND]
- Interviewer role: [RECRUITER / HIRING MANAGER / PEER / SKIP-LEVEL / EXECUTIVE]
- What I know about the company: [BRIEF SUMMARY]
- What I genuinely want to know: [YOUR REAL QUESTIONS/CONCERNS]

Generate questions in these categories:

**About the Role (3 questions):**
- Questions that show I'm already thinking about succeeding in the role

**About the Team (3 questions):**
- Questions about team dynamics, collaboration, and structure

**About the Company (3 questions):**
- Questions that demonstrate I've done research and am thinking strategically

**About Growth (2 questions):**
- Questions about career development, learning, and advancement

**Insightful "Wow" Questions (3 questions):**
- Questions that make the interviewer pause and think — questions nobody else asks

For each question:
- The question itself
- Why it's a smart question to ask
- What you'll learn from the answer
- Who to ask it to (recruiter vs. hiring manager vs. executive)
- When NOT to ask it (some questions are wrong for certain rounds)

Also provide:
- 5 questions to NEVER ask (and why they backfire)
- The best final question to end any interview on a strong note
```

> **Pro Tip:** The questions you ask reveal more about you than the answers you give. Asking "What does a typical day look like?" is fine. Asking "What's the biggest unsolved problem on this team that the person in this role could tackle?" is memorable.

## 10. Mock Interview Simulator

**Level:** All Levels
**When to use:** You want to practice answering questions with real-time feedback.

```
Act as my interview coach and conduct a mock interview for a [JOB TITLE] position at [COMPANY NAME].

Interview type: [BEHAVIORAL / TECHNICAL / CASE STUDY / MIXED]
Difficulty: [ENTRY / MID / SENIOR / EXECUTIVE LEVEL]
Interviewer style: [FRIENDLY / NEUTRAL / CHALLENGING / RAPID-FIRE]

Job description:
[PASTE JOB DESCRIPTION]

My background (brief):
[2-3 SENTENCES ABOUT YOUR EXPERIENCE]

Here's how this works:
1. Ask me ONE interview question at a time
2. Wait for my response
3. After I answer, give me:
   - A score (1-10) for that answer
   - What was strong about my answer
   - What was weak or missing
   - A revised "ideal" answer that I can learn from
   - The next question

Start with "Tell me about yourself" and progress through 8-10 questions, getting more challenging as we go.

Mix in:
- 3 behavioral questions
- 2 situational questions
- 2 role-specific questions
- 1 curveball/unexpected question
- 1 "Do you have questions for me?" at the end

After all questions, give me an overall assessment:
- Overall score
- Top 3 strengths in my interview performance
- Top 3 areas to improve
- Specific things to practice before the real interview
```

> **Why This Works:** Practice out loud. Reading about interview prep is not the same as doing it. This prompt lets you practice with feedback at any time, for free.

## 11. Panel Interview Strategy

**Level:** Mid / Senior / Executive
**When to use:** You'll be interviewed by multiple people simultaneously.

```
Help me prepare for a panel interview with multiple interviewers for [JOB TITLE] at [COMPANY NAME].

Panel members (what I know):
1. [NAME], [TITLE] — [THEIR LIKELY PRIORITIES]
2. [NAME], [TITLE] — [THEIR LIKELY PRIORITIES]
3. [NAME], [TITLE] — [THEIR LIKELY PRIORITIES]
4. [NAME], [TITLE] — [THEIR LIKELY PRIORITIES]

If I don't know the panel yet, help me:
1. Predict who will likely be on the panel based on the role
2. Anticipate what each person cares about based on their function

Prep strategy:
1. **Reading the room** — How to address a panel vs. one person (eye contact, who to direct answers to)
2. **Tailored answers** — How to angle the same answer to satisfy different stakeholders (the hiring manager cares about execution, the VP cares about strategy, HR cares about culture)
3. **Name usage** — How to use panelists' names naturally
4. **Energy management** — How to stay engaging across 60-90 minutes with multiple people
5. **Difficult dynamics** — What to do if panelists disagree with each other, if one person dominates, or if someone seems disengaged
6. **Individual follow-ups** — Should I send separate thank-you notes to each panelist?

Also generate 3 questions I can ask that engage the whole panel (not just one person).
```

**When to use:** Preparing for the initial recruiter or hiring manager phone screen.

```

1. **Expected questions and scripted answers:**
   - "Tell me about yourself" (30-second version for phone)

   - "What's your salary expectation?" (how to handle this)
   - "What's your availability/timeline?"
   - "Why are you leaving your current role?"

2. **Logistics prep:**
   - Where to take the call (environment checklist)

   - Speaking pace and energy on phone (different from in-person)
   - How to handle "Can you hold?" or technical difficulties

3. **Screening questions I might face:**
   - Deal-breaker questions (location, salary, start date, travel, visa status)
   - How to answer without disqualifying myself prematurely
   - Which questions to answer directly vs. defer to later rounds

4. **Red flags to listen for:**
   - Warning signs about the role, team, or company
   - Questions that suggest a problem with the role

5. **Closing strong:**
   - How to end the phone screen to maximize chances of advancing
   - What to ask about next steps and timeline
   - Follow-up email template to send within 2 hours

My background:
- Current role: [TITLE at COMPANY]
- Key relevant experience: [2-3 SENTENCES]
- Salary range: [YOUR RANGE]
- Availability: [WHEN YOU COULD START]
```

## 17. Career Gap Interview Answers

**Level:** All Levels
**When to use:** You have a gap in your resume and expect questions about it.

```
Help me prepare a confident interview answer for questions about my career gap.

My gap:
- Duration: [LENGTH]
- When: [DATES]
- Reason: [HONEST REASON]
- What I did during the gap (if anything relevant): [ACTIVITIES]
- What I learned: [INSIGHTS OR SKILLS GAINED]

Write answers for these variations:
1. "I notice a gap in your resume. Can you tell me about that?"
2. "What were you doing between [DATE] and [DATE]?"
3. "Why weren't you working during this period?"

For each:
- Keep the explanation to 2-3 sentences MAX (don't over-explain)
- Be honest without oversharing personal details
- Pivot quickly to what I learned or how I grew
- End on a forward-looking, positive note
- Show that I'm fully ready and committed now
- Never apologize for the gap

Also provide:
```

- [ ] Lay out your outfit (if in-person) or check your video setup (if remote)

- [ ] Get a good night's sleep

**1 Hour Before:**
- [ ] Review your notes and STAR stories
- [ ] Do a confidence exercise (use Prompt #25)
- [ ] Have water, a pen, and notepad ready
- [ ] Turn off all notifications
- [ ] Arrive 10 minutes early (in-person) or log in 5 minutes early (remote)

**During:**
- [ ] Make eye contact, smile, breathe
- [ ] Listen fully before answering
- [ ] Use names when possible
- [ ] Take notes on important points
- [ ] Ask your prepared questions

- [ ] Send thank-you emails (use Prompt #20)
