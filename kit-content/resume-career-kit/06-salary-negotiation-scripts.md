## 1. Initial Offer Response

**Level:** All Levels
**When to use:** You just received a job offer and need to respond without immediately accepting or countering.

```
I just received a job offer and need a script for my initial response. I do NOT want to accept immediately — I want to buy time to evaluate and prepare a counter-offer.

The offer:
- Company: [COMPANY NAME]
- Role: [JOB TITLE]
- Base salary: [$AMOUNT]
- Bonus: [$AMOUNT OR PERCENTAGE]
- Equity: [DETAILS IF APPLICABLE]
- Benefits: [WHAT THEY MENTIONED]
- Start date: [DATE]
- Deadline to respond: [DATE, if given]

How the offer was delivered: [PHONE CALL / EMAIL / IN PERSON / THROUGH RECRUITER]

My initial reaction: [EXCITED / NEUTRAL / DISAPPOINTED]

Write scripts for:

**1. Phone/verbal response (what to say in the moment):**
- Express genuine enthusiasm (even if the number is low — never react negatively)
- Thank them specifically
- Buy time without being evasive: "I'm very excited about this. I'd like to take a couple of days to review everything carefully."
- Ask for the offer in writing if not already provided
- Confirm the response deadline

**2. If they pressure for an immediate answer:**
- "I always make important decisions thoughtfully, and I want to give this the consideration it deserves."
- Alternative phrases that buy time without raising red flags

**3. Email response (if the offer came by email):**
- Thank them
- Express excitement
- Confirm you'll review and respond by [DATE]
- Under 100 words

CRITICAL RULES:
- Never say yes or no in the first conversation
- Never reveal your reaction to the number (positive or negative)
- Never say "That's lower than I expected" — you lose leverage
- Never lie about having other offers
- Express enthusiasm for the ROLE, not the OFFER (there's a difference)
```

> **Why This Works:** The initial response sets the tone for the entire negotiation. Accepting immediately leaves money on the table. Reacting negatively puts them on the defensive. This script threads the needle perfectly.

> **Pro Tip:** Even if the offer is exactly what you wanted, always take 24-48 hours. You can still negotiate other terms (signing bonus, start date, remote days, title) even if the salary is perfect.

## 2. Counter-Offer Script

**Level:** All Levels
**When to use:** You've reviewed the offer and want to negotiate a higher salary or better terms.

```
Help me build a counter-offer script for my job offer negotiation.

The offer I received:
- Base salary: [$AMOUNT]
- Bonus: [$AMOUNT]
- Other comp: [EQUITY, BENEFITS, ETC.]
- Total package value: [$ESTIMATED TOTAL]

My target:
- Target base salary: [$AMOUNT]
- My justification: [WHY I BELIEVE I'M WORTH MORE — market data, experience, competing offers, cost of living, etc.]
- My BATNA (best alternative): [MY BACKUP IF THEY SAY NO — another offer, staying at current job, etc.]
- Am I willing to walk away? [YES / NO / DEPENDS]

Market data I have:
- Glassdoor range for this role: [$RANGE]
- Levels.fyi/Payscale data: [$RANGE]
- What colleagues in similar roles earn: [$RANGE, if known]
- My current salary: [$AMOUNT]

Write counter-offer scripts for:

**1. Phone script (recommended for counter-offers):**

Opening:
"Thank you again for the offer. I'm very excited about joining [COMPANY] as [ROLE]. I've had a chance to review the full package, and I'd love to discuss the compensation."

The ask:
[Help me frame my counter-offer amount with supporting rationale]

The close:
[How to end the conversation and what to expect next]

**2. Email script (if phone isn't possible):**
- Full email with subject line
- Same structure: gratitude → enthusiasm → counter → rationale → flexibility

**3. If they ask "What are you looking for?":**
- Never give a single number — give a range (with your target at the bottom)
- The exact phrasing to use

**4. If they say "This is the best we can do" (first response):**
- How to test if it's truly final or just a negotiation tactic
- Alternative asks: "If the base salary is firm, can we discuss [signing bonus / equity / title / PTO]?"

**5. If they actually can't move on salary:**
- Pivot script to negotiate other terms (see Prompt #3)
- How to gracefully accept without sounding defeated

NEGOTIATION PRINCIPLES:
- Always negotiate over the phone when possible (email loses nuance)
- Lead with gratitude and enthusiasm before the ask
- Use "we" language ("I'm hoping we can find a number that works for both of us")
- Give reasons for your ask (backed by data, not feelings)
- Never give an ultimatum unless you're prepared to follow through
- Silence is a tool — after stating your ask, stop talking
```

> **Pro Tip:** The counter-offer conversation is typically 5-10 minutes. That's potentially $10,000+ per minute of discomfort. Every dollar you negotiate in base salary compounds over your entire career.

## 3. Benefits Negotiation

**Level:** All Levels
**When to use:** When salary is fixed or you want to improve the total package beyond base pay.

```
The base salary is either fixed or I've negotiated it as far as it'll go. Help me negotiate the rest of the compensation package.

My offer:
- Base salary: [$AMOUNT — final or near-final]
- Current benefits offered: [LIST WHAT THEY'VE OFFERED]

What I want to negotiate:
[CHECK ALL THAT APPLY AND FILL IN DETAILS]
- [ ] Signing bonus: [TARGET AMOUNT]
- [ ] Annual bonus: [TARGET PERCENTAGE]
- [ ] Stock/equity: [TARGET]
- [ ] Extra PTO/vacation days: [TARGET DAYS]
- [ ] Remote work days: [TARGET DAYS PER WEEK]
- [ ] Professional development budget: [TARGET AMOUNT]
- [ ] Tuition reimbursement: [AMOUNT]
- [ ] Relocation assistance: [AMOUNT]
- [ ] Home office stipend: [AMOUNT]
- [ ] Title upgrade: [TARGET TITLE]
- [ ] Earlier performance review: [TARGET — e.g., "6 months instead of 12"]
- [ ] Accelerated vesting schedule: [FOR EQUITY]
- [ ] Flexible hours: [SPECIFIC ASK]
- [ ] Other: [ANYTHING ELSE]

For each item I want to negotiate, write:
1. The exact ask script (what to say)
2. The rationale (why it's reasonable)
3. The fallback (if they say no to this, what's the next ask?)
4. Approximate monetary value (to calculate total comp impact)

Also provide:
- The ORDER in which to negotiate these items (strategy matters)
- Which items are typically easiest for companies to say yes to
- Which items have the biggest long-term value (some are worth more than salary increases)
- A "total compensation" calculation showing the monetary value of all benefits combined
```

> **Why This Works:** Most candidates only negotiate salary. But a $5K signing bonus, 5 extra PTO days, and a $2K home office stipend can easily be worth $10K+ in total value. Companies often have more flexibility on benefits than base salary because benefits come from different budget lines.

## 4. Competing Offers Leverage

**Level:** Mid / Senior / Executive
**When to use:** You have another offer (or strong interest) and want to use it ethically to strengthen your negotiation.

```
I have competing offers/interest and want to use this leverage in my negotiation. Help me do this ethically and effectively.

My situation:
- Offer A: [COMPANY, ROLE, TOTAL COMP]
- Offer B: [COMPANY, ROLE, TOTAL COMP]
- Strong interest from: [ANY COMPANIES IN LATE-STAGE INTERVIEWS]
- My preferred company: [WHICH ONE I'D CHOOSE IF COMP WERE EQUAL]
- The gap: [WHAT PREFERRED COMPANY NEEDS TO OFFER FOR ME TO CHOOSE THEM]

Write scripts for:

**1. Telling your preferred company about the competing offer:**
"I want to be transparent with you. I've received another offer from [COMPANY B or 'another company in the space']. I'm genuinely more excited about [PREFERRED COMPANY] because [SPECIFIC REASON], but I want to make sure we're aligned on compensation."

**2. Handling "How much are they offering?":**
- When to share the number (only if it helps you)
- When to keep it vague ("It's a competitive offer in line with market rates")
- How to answer without lying if their offer is actually lower

**3. Creating urgency without ultimatums:**
- "I have a deadline of [DATE] to respond to [Company B]"
- How to use time pressure ethically

**4. If they ask you to prove the other offer:**
- You're never obligated to show an offer letter
- Script: "I'd prefer to keep the specifics confidential, but I'm happy to tell you what I need to make this work."

**5. If they can't match but you still want them:**
- How to negotiate non-salary terms to close the gap
- How to accept a lower offer without feeling like you lost

ETHICAL GUIDELINES:
- Never fabricate an offer you don't have
- Never share confidential offer details (dollar amounts are okay, offer letters are not)
- Don't play companies against each other maliciously
- Be honest about your preference — it builds trust
- Remember: you'll work with these people. Don't poison the relationship before day 1.
```

> **Pro Tip:** You don't need a competing offer to negotiate. But if you have one, it's the strongest leverage that exists. Use it ethically and you can often close a 10-20% gap.

## 5. Equity/Stock Negotiation

**Level:** Mid / Senior / Executive
**When to use:** Your offer includes equity (stock options, RSUs, etc.) and you need to evaluate and negotiate it.

```
Help me understand and negotiate the equity component of my job offer.

My equity offer:
- Type: [STOCK OPTIONS (ISO/NSO) / RSUs / PROFIT SHARING / PHANTOM EQUITY / OTHER]
- Number of shares/units: [NUMBER]
- Strike price (if options): [$PRICE]
- Current share price or valuation: [$PRICE OR VALUATION]
- Vesting schedule: [TYPICAL: 4 years with 1-year cliff]
- Company stage: [PRE-SEED / SEED / SERIES A / B / C / PUBLIC]
- Total shares outstanding (if known): [NUMBER — determines your percentage]

Help me:

1. **Understand what I actually have:**
   - What is my equity worth TODAY? (And why "potential value" is different from "actual value")
   - What percentage of the company do I own?
   - What could it be worth at various exit scenarios?
   - What's the realistic probability of each scenario?

2. **Evaluate the offer:**
   - Is this generous, average, or low for my role and level?
   - Benchmark against industry standards for [JOB TITLE] at a [COMPANY STAGE] company
   - Red flags in the equity structure I should watch for

3. **Negotiate more equity:**
   - Script for asking for more shares
   - Arguments that work: "Given that the base is below market at $[X], I'd like to see the equity component compensate for that gap"
   - What's typically negotiable: share count, vesting schedule, acceleration clauses

4. **Questions to ask before signing:**
   - Tax implications (409A valuation, exercise window, AMT)
   - What happens to my equity if I leave? (Exercise window is critical)
   - What happens in acquisition, IPO, or down round?
   - Is there a buyback or secondary sale option?

5. **Key terms to negotiate:**
   - Extended exercise window (standard 90 days is terrible — ask for 5-10 years)
   - Acceleration on change of control (single vs. double trigger)
   - Refresh grants and promotion grants
```

## 6. Signing Bonus Request

**Level:** All Levels
**When to use:** You want to request a signing bonus, especially when base salary is fixed.

```
Help me negotiate a signing bonus as part of my job offer.

My situation:
- Base salary offered: [$AMOUNT]
- Base salary I wanted: [$AMOUNT]
- The gap: [$AMOUNT]
- Am I leaving money on the table at my current job? [YES — unvested equity, pending bonus, etc. / NO]
- Relocation required? [YES / NO]
- Other justification: [ANY REASON A SIGNING BONUS MAKES SENSE]

Write scripts for requesting a signing bonus in different scenarios:

**1. To bridge a salary gap:**
"I understand the base salary is at $[X]. To bridge the gap to my target, would the team be open to a signing bonus of $[Y]? This would help offset [SPECIFIC REASON] while keeping within the band for this role."

**2. To offset what I'm leaving behind:**
"By joining [COMPANY], I'll be forfeiting [$AMOUNT] in [unvested equity / pending bonus / accrued PTO] at my current employer. A signing bonus of $[Y] would help make this transition whole."

**3. To cover relocation:**
"The relocation from [CITY A] to [CITY B] will involve significant costs. Would a signing bonus or relocation assistance of $[Y] be possible?"

**4. When they say "We don't typically do signing bonuses":**
- "I understand. Is there another way we could address [the gap / the forfeited comp / the relocation cost]? I'm flexible on the structure."

**5. Negotiating the clawback:**
- Most signing bonuses have a clawback clause (you repay if you leave within 12-24 months)
- How to negotiate: shorter clawback period or prorated repayment
- What's reasonable to accept

Pro tips:
- Signing bonuses are often easier to get than salary increases (they're one-time costs, not recurring)
- Always ask for a signing bonus if you're leaving unvested compensation
- A signing bonus doesn't increase your base for future raises, so get salary right first
```

## 7. Remote Work Negotiation

**Level:** All Levels
**When to use:** You want to negotiate remote or hybrid work arrangements.

```
Help me negotiate remote work flexibility as part of my job offer or current role.

My situation:
- What the role is listed as: [REMOTE / HYBRID / IN-OFFICE]
- What I want: [FULLY REMOTE / HYBRID WITH X DAYS / SPECIFIC ARRANGEMENT]
- My justification: [WHY — e.g., "I perform better remotely," "I have a long commute," "I'm in a different city," "I have caregiving responsibilities"]
- My leverage: [STRONG — THEY WANT ME / MODERATE / LOW]
- My track record: [YEARS WORKING REMOTELY / SPECIFIC ACCOMPLISHMENTS WHILE REMOTE]

Write negotiation scripts for:

**1. During offer negotiation:**
"I'm very excited about this role. One thing that would make this a perfect fit is the flexibility to work remotely [X days per week / fully remote]. In my current role, I've [SPECIFIC REMOTE WORK ACHIEVEMENT], and I find I'm most productive when [YOUR ARGUMENT]."

**2. If they say "This role requires in-office presence":**
- "I understand. Would a trial period be possible? I could start [in-office / hybrid] and we could revisit after 3 months based on my performance."
- Fallback: negotiate the number of in-office days

**3. If remote work affects salary:**
- Some companies pay differently based on location
- How to negotiate if they try to reduce salary for remote work
- "I'd be delivering the same value regardless of location" argument and its limits

**4. Structuring the arrangement:**
- What to get in writing (don't trust verbal agreements on remote work)
- Specific terms to define: core hours, in-person requirements, equipment stipend
- How to handle "subject to change" clauses

**5. For existing employees requesting remote flexibility:**
- How to build the case with your current manager
- Data points to present (productivity metrics, peer benchmarks)
- Timing the ask (after a big win, during review cycle)
```

## 8. Title Negotiation

**Level:** Mid / Senior / Executive
**When to use:** The title they're offering doesn't match your experience or career trajectory.

```
Help me negotiate a better job title as part of my offer.

The offer:
- Title offered: [TITLE]
- Title I want: [TARGET TITLE]
- Why the upgrade matters: [CAREER PROGRESSION / EXTERNAL CREDIBILITY / INTERNAL INFLUENCE / FUTURE JOB SEARCHES]

My justification:
- My current title: [CURRENT TITLE]
- My experience level: [YEARS AND SCOPE]
- What competitors call this role: [TITLES AT OTHER COMPANIES]
- What the role actually involves: [SCOPE OF RESPONSIBILITIES — does it match a senior/lead/director level?]

Write scripts for:

**1. Requesting a title upgrade:**
"Looking at the scope of this role — [DESCRIBE SCOPE: team size, budget, strategic importance] — I believe a [TARGET TITLE] better reflects the level of responsibility and would align with industry standards. Would there be flexibility on the title?"

**2. If they say titles are standardized:**
"I understand you have a leveling framework. Could you help me understand what level this role falls into? Given my [X] years of experience and the scope of the role, I want to make sure I'm entering at the right level for growth."

**3. If they offer title-in-lieu-of-salary:**
- When a better title is actually a good deal (especially if you'll job search again in 2-3 years)
- When it's a consolation prize to avoid paying more
- How to evaluate

**4. External-facing vs. internal title:**
- Some companies allow different titles on business cards/LinkedIn than in their HR system
- "Would it be possible to use [TARGET TITLE] externally while maintaining [OFFERED TITLE] internally?"

Why titles matter:
- Your next job search starts with your current title
- Titles affect who takes your calls, who accepts your meeting requests, and what roles recruiters pitch you
- Going backward in title (even for more money) can set your career back by 2-3 years
```

## 9. Start Date Negotiation

**Level:** All Levels
**When to use:** You need more time before starting or want to negotiate the start date.

```
Help me negotiate my start date without endangering the offer.

My situation:
- Their requested start date: [DATE]
- My preferred start date: [DATE]
- Why I need more time: [REASON — two-week notice, vacation planned, personal commitment, relocation, mental health break between jobs]
- How much flexibility they likely have: [HIGH / MODERATE / LOW]

Write scripts for:

**1. Requesting a later start date:**
"I'm absolutely committed to this role and want to start strong. To ensure a smooth transition, I'd like to propose a start date of [DATE]. This gives me time to [REASON] so I can hit the ground running on day one."

**2. If they push back:**
- "I understand the urgency. Could we compromise at [MIDDLE DATE]?"
- "I can start doing onboarding tasks (paperwork, reading materials) before my official start date if that would help."

**3. Protecting a planned vacation:**
- "I have a pre-planned commitment from [DATE] to [DATE]. I'd like to either start after that or start before and take those days off in my first month. Which works better for the team?"
- Always disclose planned PTO before accepting — it's much harder to negotiate after you start.

**4. Two-week notice issues:**
- Script for explaining you need to give proper notice
- Why any good employer respects this (it shows loyalty, which benefits them too)

**5. Negotiating a gap between jobs:**
- It's okay to take 1-2 weeks off between jobs
- "I've found that a brief reset between roles helps me start with full energy. Would a start date of [DATE] work?"
```

## 10. Raise Request Script

**Level:** All Levels
**When to use:** You want to ask your current employer for a raise (not related to a new offer).

```
Help me build a case and script for requesting a raise at my current company.

My situation:
- Current role: [JOB TITLE]
- Current salary: [$AMOUNT]
- Time in role: [DURATION]
- Last raise: [WHEN AND HOW MUCH]
- Target raise: [$AMOUNT OR PERCENTAGE]
- Company's financial situation: [GROWING / STABLE / STRUGGLING / UNSURE]
- My manager: [SUPPORTIVE / NEUTRAL / DIFFICULT]
- Is it review season? [YES / NO — reviews happen in MONTH]
- Flight risk: Am I willing to leave? [YES / NO]

My case for a raise:

1. Accomplishments since last raise:
   - [ACHIEVEMENT WITH METRICS]
   - [ACHIEVEMENT WITH METRICS]
   - [ACHIEVEMENT WITH METRICS]

2. Expanded responsibilities:
   - [THINGS I DO NOW THAT WEREN'T IN MY ORIGINAL ROLE]

3. Market data:
   - [WHAT COMPARABLE ROLES PAY — from Glassdoor, Levels.fyi, Payscale, or industry data]

4. Additional leverage:
   - [COMPETING OFFERS, RECRUITER INTEREST, CRITICAL PROJECTS I OWN, ETC.]

Write:

**1. Meeting request email:**
"I'd like to schedule 30 minutes to discuss my compensation. I've prepared some thoughts I'd like to share."

**2. The conversation script:**

Opening: [Express that I value this role and want to grow here]

The case: [Present accomplishments, expanded scope, and market data]

The ask: [State the specific number/percentage confidently]

The close: [What to say after making the ask — then be quiet]

**3. If they say "The budget isn't there right now":**
- "I understand. Can we set a specific date and target for revisiting this?"
- "What milestones would I need to hit for a [X%] increase?"
- Get a written commitment with a timeline

**4. If they say "You're already well-compensated":**
- How to push back respectfully with data
- When to accept this response vs. when to start looking elsewhere

**5. Follow-up email summarizing the conversation:**
- Confirm what was agreed (even if it's "we'll revisit in Q3")
- Create a paper trail
```

> **Why This Works:** The #1 reason people don't get raises is they don't ask. The #2 reason is they ask without data. This prompt solves both problems.

## 11. Promotion Case Builder

**Level:** Mid / Senior
**When to use:** You want to build a compelling case for a promotion at your current company.

```
Help me build an airtight case for a promotion at my current company.

- Current title: [TITLE]
- Target title: [TARGET TITLE]

My promotion case:

1. **I'm already doing the job:**
   List specific responsibilities I currently perform that are part of the next level:
   - [RESPONSIBILITY THAT'S ABOVE MY CURRENT LEVEL]
   - [RESPONSIBILITY THAT'S ABOVE MY CURRENT LEVEL]
   - [RESPONSIBILITY THAT'S ABOVE MY CURRENT LEVEL]

2. **My achievements at the current level:**
   - [ACHIEVEMENT WITH METRICS — exceeding expectations]
   - [ACHIEVEMENT WITH METRICS]
   - [ACHIEVEMENT WITH METRICS]

3. **Impact beyond my role:**
   - [CROSS-FUNCTIONAL CONTRIBUTIONS]
   - [MENTORSHIP OR TEAM DEVELOPMENT]
   - [PROCESS IMPROVEMENTS]

4. **Peer comparison (careful here):**
   - [WITHOUT BEING POLITICAL: what level are people doing similar work at?]

2. Script the conversation with my manager

## 12. Total Compensation Analyzer

**Level:** All Levels
**When to use:** You need to compare job offers beyond just base salary.

```
Help me calculate and compare the total compensation of my job offers (or my current role vs. a new offer).

Option A — [COMPANY A / CURRENT ROLE]:
- Base salary: [$AMOUNT]
- Annual bonus: [$AMOUNT OR % — guaranteed? performance-based?]
- Signing bonus: [$AMOUNT]
- Stock/equity: [DETAILS — RSUs, options, vesting schedule, current value]
- 401(k) match: [DETAILS]
- Health insurance: [MONTHLY PREMIUM, DEDUCTIBLE, HSA CONTRIBUTION]
- PTO days: [NUMBER]
- Other benefits: [TUITION REIMBURSEMENT, COMMUTER BENEFITS, WELLNESS STIPEND, ETC.]
- Remote/commute: [FULLY REMOTE / COMMUTE COST PER MONTH]
- Location cost of living: [CITY]

Option B — [COMPANY B / NEW OFFER]:
[SAME FIELDS AS ABOVE]

Option C (if applicable): [SAME FIELDS]

Help me:

1. **Calculate total annual compensation for each option:**
   - Base + guaranteed bonus + estimated variable bonus + annualized equity + employer benefits value
   - Include the monetary value of PTO days (daily rate x extra days)
   - Include commute savings for remote roles
   - Account for cost of living differences if locations differ

2. **Build a comparison table:**
   - Side-by-side with all components
   - Total annual value
   - 4-year total (important for equity vesting)

3. **Highlight hidden differences:**
   - Which benefits have the most value I might be overlooking?
   - Tax implications (RSUs vs. ISOs, state tax differences, 401k match limits)
   - Growth trajectory (which role likely leads to faster salary growth?)

4. **Make a recommendation:**
   - If money is my top priority: which option wins?
   - If career growth is my top priority: which option wins?
   - If work-life balance is my top priority: which option wins?
   - Overall: what would you recommend, and why?
```

> **Pro Tip:** Two offers that look identical in base salary can differ by $30K+ in total compensation when you factor in equity, bonuses, benefits, and cost of living. Always compare total comp, not base salary.

## 14. Handling Pressure Tactics

**Level:** All Levels
**When to use:** When the employer uses pressure tactics during negotiation.

```
Help me identify and respond to common employer pressure tactics during salary negotiation.

My situation:
- Role: [JOB TITLE] at [COMPANY NAME]
- Offer: [$AMOUNT]
- My counter: [$AMOUNT]
- Pressure I'm feeling: [DESCRIBE THE SITUATION]

Write responses for these common pressure tactics:

**1. "We need an answer by tomorrow."**
(Exploding offer / artificial urgency)
Response: "I appreciate the timeline. I want to make a thoughtful decision that's right for both of us. Could we extend to [DATE]? I'm very interested but want to make sure I can commit fully."
When it's real urgency vs. a tactic: [How to tell the difference]

**2. "This is our final offer."**
(Might be true, might not be)

## Salary Negotiation Rules

1. **Never accept the first offer.** Companies expect you to negotiate. The first number has room built in.

2. **Never share your current salary.** In many jurisdictions, they can't legally ask. Focus on market value, not what you currently earn.

3. **Let them say the number first.** The first person to say a number sets the anchor. If pressed, give a range with your target at the bottom.

4. **Use data, not feelings.** "Glassdoor shows the range for this role is $X-$Y" is stronger than "I feel like I deserve more."

5. **Negotiate over the phone.** Email loses nuance and makes it easy for them to say no. Phone conversations create human connection.

6. **After making your ask, stop talking.** Silence is your most powerful tool. State your number and wait. The urge to fill the silence is strong — resist it.

7. **Never bluff.** Don't claim you have an offer you don't have. Don't threaten to walk away unless you mean it.

8. **Get everything in writing.** Verbal promises disappear. If it's not in the offer letter, it doesn't exist.

9. **Be likable.** Negotiation isn't combat. The goal is a deal that makes both sides feel good. You're going to work with these people.

10. **Know your BATNA.** Best Alternative To Negotiated Agreement. If you have a strong backup plan, you negotiate from strength. If you don't, know that and adjust.
