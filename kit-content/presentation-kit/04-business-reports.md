# 04 — Business Reports & Reviews Prompts

## 20+ AI Prompts for Business Presentations That Drive Decisions

These prompts help you create data-driven business presentations — quarterly reviews, board meetings, strategy decks, all-hands meetings, and project updates. Each prompt generates structured content with clear data storytelling, executive-ready formatting, and actionable recommendations.

**Works with:** ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, or any LLM.

## Quarterly Business Reviews

### Prompt 1 — Quarterly Business Review (QBR) Presentation

Use this to create a comprehensive quarterly review for your leadership team or stakeholders.

```
You are a business analytics leader who presents quarterly reviews to executive teams. Create a quarterly business review presentation.

Company/Department: [COMPANY OR DEPARTMENT NAME]
Quarter: [Q1/Q2/Q3/Q4 YEAR]
Reporting to: [WHO WILL SEE THIS — e.g., "CEO + leadership team," "board of directors," "VP of Operations"]

Key metrics this quarter:
- Revenue: [AMOUNT — vs. target of $TARGET, vs. last quarter $LAST_Q]
- Profit/Margin: [AMOUNT OR %]
- Customer metrics: [NEW CUSTOMERS, CHURN, NPS, RETENTION]
- Operational metrics: [RELEVANT OPS METRICS — efficiency, throughput, SLA compliance]
- Team metrics: [HEADCOUNT, HIRING, ATTRITION]
- Budget: [SPEND vs. BUDGET — OVER/UNDER BY HOW MUCH]

Key wins this quarter: [LIST 3-5 NOTABLE ACHIEVEMENTS]
Key challenges: [LIST 2-3 MAIN CHALLENGES OR MISSES]
Strategic initiatives status: [LIST INITIATIVES AND STATUS]
Next quarter priorities: [TOP 3-5 PRIORITIES]

Generate a 12-slide QBR presentation:

1. **Executive Summary Slide**
   - Overall health status: GREEN / YELLOW / RED
   - 3-sentence summary: what went well, what needs attention, what's next
   - Key number: the single most important metric this quarter

2. **Scorecard Slide**
   - Table format: Metric | Target | Actual | vs. Prior Quarter | Status
   - Color-coded for quick scanning (green/yellow/red indicators described)
   - 8-10 key metrics

3. **Revenue Deep Dive Slide**
   - Revenue trend (describe chart: monthly progression)
   - Revenue by segment/product/region
   - Pipeline analysis for next quarter
   - Commentary on growth trajectory

4. **Customer Metrics Slide**
   - New customers acquired (vs. target)
   - Customer retention rate
   - NPS or satisfaction trend
   - Churn analysis with root causes

5. **Operational Performance Slide**
   - Key operational metrics with trends
   - Efficiency improvements or declines
   - SLA compliance
   - Quality metrics

6. **Key Wins Slide**
   - 3-5 notable wins with business impact quantified
   - Brief context for each (what happened and why it matters)
   - Team/individual recognition

7. **Challenges & Lessons Slide**
   - 2-3 misses or challenges, each with:
     - What happened
     - Root cause
     - Action taken
     - Lessons learned

8. **Strategic Initiatives Update**
   - Initiative | Status | Progress | Next Milestone | Risk Level
   - For each initiative: one sentence on current status

9. **Financial Summary Slide**
   - Budget vs. actual spending
   - Key variances explained
   - Forecast for remainder of year (if applicable)

10. **Team & Organization Slide**
    - Headcount: current vs. plan
    - Key hires made
    - Open positions
    - Team health indicators (engagement, retention)

11. **Next Quarter Plan Slide**
    - Top 3-5 priorities ranked
    - For each: objective, key results, owner, timeline
    - Resources needed

12. **Discussion Slide**
    - 2-3 specific decisions needed from leadership
    - Open questions for the group
    - Support or resources being requested

Speaker notes should be crisp and insight-driven. For each metric, explain WHY, not just WHAT. "Revenue was $1.2M" is a fact. "Revenue was $1.2M, 15% above target, driven by strong enterprise deal closure in the last 3 weeks of the quarter" is an insight.
```

### Prompt 2 — Department-Level Quarterly Review

Use this for a functional department review (marketing, engineering, HR, etc.) reporting to your VP or C-level.

```
You are a department leader presenting quarterly results to your VP or C-suite. Create a department quarterly review.

Department: [DEPARTMENT NAME — e.g., Marketing, Engineering, Customer Success, HR, Finance]
Department head: [YOUR NAME AND TITLE]
Reporting to: [WHO — e.g., "VP of Revenue," "CTO," "COO"]
Quarter: [Q1/Q2/Q3/Q4 YEAR]
Team size: [NUMBER OF PEOPLE]

Department OKRs/goals this quarter:
- Goal 1: [OBJECTIVE — TARGET — ACTUAL RESULT]
- Goal 2: [OBJECTIVE — TARGET — ACTUAL RESULT]
- Goal 3: [OBJECTIVE — TARGET — ACTUAL RESULT]

Key projects completed: [LIST 3-5]
Projects in progress: [LIST 2-3]
Projects delayed or at risk: [LIST ANY]

Department-specific metrics:
- [METRIC 1: NAME — VALUE — TREND]
- [METRIC 2: NAME — VALUE — TREND]
- [METRIC 3: NAME — VALUE — TREND]
- [METRIC 4: NAME — VALUE — TREND]
- [METRIC 5: NAME — VALUE — TREND]

Budget: [ALLOCATED — SPENT — VARIANCE]
Headcount: [PLANNED — ACTUAL — OPEN ROLES]
Biggest challenge: [WHAT'S HOLDING THE TEAM BACK]
Biggest opportunity: [WHAT COULD ACCELERATE RESULTS]

Generate a 10-slide department review:

1. **Department Snapshot** — One-slide overview: health status, top metric, one-sentence summary
2. **OKR Scorecard** — Goal-by-goal assessment with actuals vs. targets
3. **Key Metrics Dashboard** — 5-7 department-specific metrics with trends
4. **Project Portfolio** — Status of all major projects (completed, in progress, planned)
5. **Wins & Impact** — Top achievements with business impact quantified
6. **Challenges & Risks** — Honest assessment with root causes and mitigation
7. **Budget Review** — Spending vs. plan with variance explanations
8. **Team Update** — Headcount, hiring, team health, development
9. **Next Quarter OKRs** — Proposed objectives and key results for Q+1
10. **Asks** — What you need from leadership: budget, headcount, decisions, support

Speaker notes should anticipate tough questions from your VP/C-suite. For each underperformance, have the root cause and recovery plan ready.
```

### Prompt 3 — Monthly Business Update Presentation

Use this for a more frequent (monthly) update that's shorter and faster-paced than a quarterly review.

```
You are a business operations leader who runs efficient monthly updates. Create a monthly business update.

Company/Team: [NAME]
Month: [MONTH YEAR]
Audience: [WHO SEES THIS]
Meeting duration: [15 MIN / 30 MIN]

Key metrics (month over month):
- [METRIC 1]: [THIS MONTH] vs. [LAST MONTH] ([CHANGE %])
- [METRIC 2]: [THIS MONTH] vs. [LAST MONTH] ([CHANGE %])
- [METRIC 3]: [THIS MONTH] vs. [LAST MONTH] ([CHANGE %])
- [METRIC 4]: [THIS MONTH] vs. [LAST MONTH] ([CHANGE %])

What went well: [2-3 HIGHLIGHTS]
What needs attention: [1-2 CONCERNS]
Blockers: [ANYTHING STALLING PROGRESS]
Key decisions needed: [ANY DECISIONS FROM THE AUDIENCE]

Generate a 6-slide monthly update:

1. **Traffic Light Summary** — One slide, three sections:
   - GREEN: What's on track (bullet points)
   - YELLOW: What needs attention (bullet points)
   - RED: What's at risk (bullet points)

2. **Key Metrics Slide** — 4-6 metrics in dashboard format:
   - Metric name, current value, trend arrow, vs. target, vs. last month
   - One sentence of commentary per metric

3. **Highlights Slide** — 2-3 wins with brief impact description

4. **Issues & Actions Slide** — Table format:
   - Issue | Impact | Action | Owner | Due Date

5. **Focus for Next Month** — Top 3 priorities:
   - Priority | Success metric | Owner

6. **Discussion / Decisions** — What you need from the group

Speaker notes should keep everything to 2-3 minutes per slide maximum. Monthly updates should be efficient — present the exceptions, not the routine.
```

## Board Meeting Presentations

### Prompt 4 — Board of Directors Presentation

Use this to create a comprehensive board meeting presentation that covers all the areas board members expect to see.

```
You are a CEO preparing for a board meeting. Create a board presentation that is thorough yet respectful of board members' time.

Company: [COMPANY NAME]
Board meeting date: [DATE]
Period covered: [QUARTER OR TIMEFRAME]
Board composition: [e.g., "3 investor directors, 2 independent directors, CEO, CFO"]

Financial performance:
- Revenue: [AMOUNT — vs. plan]
- Burn rate: [$MONTHLY BURN]
- Runway: [MONTHS]
- Cash position: [$AMOUNT]
- ARR/MRR: [IF APPLICABLE]
- Key financial metrics: [GROSS MARGIN, NET BURN, ETC.]

Business performance:
- Customer metrics: [KEY CUSTOMER DATA]
- Product metrics: [KEY PRODUCT DATA]
- Go-to-market: [SALES/MARKETING PERFORMANCE]
- Team: [HEADCOUNT, KEY HIRES, ATTRITION]

Strategic topics for discussion:
- [TOPIC 1 — e.g., "Should we expand internationally?"]
- [TOPIC 2 — e.g., "Pricing strategy change"]
- [TOPIC 3 — e.g., "Fundraising timing"]

Generate a 15-slide board deck:

1. **Agenda** — Clear agenda with time allocations
2. **CEO Summary** — 1 slide, 4-5 bullets: state of the business in plain language
3. **Key Metrics Dashboard** — The 8-10 numbers the board cares about most, with traffic light status
4. **Financial Performance** — Revenue, costs, margins, cash position with commentary
5. **Revenue Deep Dive** — Revenue by segment, growth drivers, pipeline health
6. **Customer Update** — Acquisition, retention, satisfaction, notable wins/losses
7. **Product Update** — What shipped, what's next, customer feedback
8. **Go-to-Market Update** — Sales and marketing performance, channel effectiveness
9. **Team Update** — Hiring, attrition, organizational changes, culture
10. **Competitive Landscape** — What competitors are doing, market shifts
11. **Strategic Topic 1** — Context, options, recommendation, discussion questions
12. **Strategic Topic 2** — Same format
13. **Strategic Topic 3** — Same format
14. **Risk Register** — Top 5 risks with likelihood, impact, and mitigation status
15. **Next Quarter Plan** — Priorities, targets, resource needs

Appendix slides (ready if needed):
- Detailed P&L
- Cash flow forecast
- Customer cohort data
- Competitive deep dive
- Hiring plan detail

Speaker notes should be prepared for tough board questions. For every metric that's yellow or red, have a root cause analysis and recovery plan ready. Board members have limited time — lead with insights, not data.
```

### Prompt 5 — Board Meeting Financial Update

Use this specifically for the financial portion of a board meeting, with the depth that investor-directors expect.

```
You are a CFO preparing the financial section of a board presentation. Create financial update slides.

Company: [COMPANY NAME]
Period: [MONTH/QUARTER/YEAR]
Stage: [SEED / SERIES A / SERIES B / GROWTH / PUBLIC]

Financial data:
- Revenue: [AMOUNT — breakdown by source if applicable]
- Revenue plan: [$TARGET — VARIANCE %]
- COGS: [$AMOUNT]
- Gross margin: [%]
- Operating expenses: [$AMOUNT — breakdown: R&D, S&M, G&A]
- EBITDA / Operating income: [$AMOUNT]
- Net burn: [$AMOUNT/MONTH]
- Cash balance: [$AMOUNT]
- Runway: [MONTHS AT CURRENT BURN]

Key financial events this period:
- [EVENT 1 — e.g., "Signed $500K enterprise deal"]
- [EVENT 2 — e.g., "Reduced burn by 15% through hiring freeze"]
- [EVENT 3 — e.g., "Secured $2M debt facility"]

Budget vs. actuals variance drivers: [KEY VARIANCES AND WHY]
Cash flow concerns: [ANY CASH FLOW ISSUES OR TRIGGERS]
Forecast changes: [ANY UPDATES TO FULL-YEAR FORECAST]

Generate 6 financial slides:

1. **Financial Summary Slide** — P&L snapshot:
   - Revenue, Gross Margin, OpEx, EBITDA
   - Actual vs. Budget vs. Prior Period
   - Key variance explanations

2. **Revenue Analysis Slide**
   - Revenue by product/segment/customer type
   - Revenue quality: recurring vs. one-time
   - Revenue growth rate trends
   - Pipeline conversion and forecast confidence

3. **Expense Analysis Slide**
   - OpEx by category (R&D, Sales & Marketing, G&A)
   - Headcount cost vs. non-headcount
   - Key variance drivers
   - Efficiency metrics (revenue per employee, CAC, etc.)

4. **Cash Flow Slide**
   - Cash bridge: Opening balance → inflows → outflows → closing balance
   - Monthly burn trend (describe chart showing last 6 months)
   - Runway analysis: current trajectory vs. budget trajectory
   - Cash milestones or triggers

5. **Forecast Update Slide**
   - Updated annual forecast (if changed from last board meeting)
   - Key assumption changes and why
   - Scenario table: best case / base case / downside
   - Breakeven analysis (when — if applicable)

6. **Financial Discussion Slide**
   - Key decisions needed (budget reallocation, hiring approval, etc.)
   - Risks that need board awareness
   - Upcoming financial events (fundraise timing, debt covenants, etc.)

Speaker notes should translate financial data into business decisions. Don't just report numbers — explain what the numbers mean for strategy and what actions they require.
```

Use this for the regular investor update you send to existing investors between board meetings.

```
You are a startup CEO who sends excellent investor updates. Create an investor update presentation (can also be an email format).

Company: [COMPANY NAME]
Period: [MONTH OR QUARTER]
Stage: [STAGE]
Last update sent: [WHEN]

Key numbers:
- MRR/ARR: [$AMOUNT — GROWTH %]
- Cash: [$AMOUNT — MONTHS OF RUNWAY]
- Customers: [COUNT — NET CHANGE]
- Team: [SIZE — NEW HIRES]
- Burn: [$AMOUNT/MONTH]

Highlights (the good):
- [WIN 1]
- [WIN 2]
- [WIN 3]

Lowlights (the honest):
- [CHALLENGE 1]
- [CHALLENGE 2]

Asks (how investors can help):
- [ASK 1 — e.g., "intro to VP Sales candidates"]
- [ASK 2 — e.g., "intro to [COMPANY NAME] for partnership"]
- [ASK 3 — e.g., "feedback on our pricing strategy"]

Generate:

1. **Presentation Format (6 slides):**
   - Slide 1: One-line summary of the period (headline)
   - Slide 2: Key metrics dashboard
   - Slide 3: Highlights with impact
   - Slide 4: Challenges with action plans
   - Slide 5: Next period goals
   - Slide 6: Specific asks for investors

2. **Email Format (alternative):**
   - Subject line
   - TL;DR (3 bullets)
   - Metrics table
   - Highlights section
   - Lowlights section
   - Asks section
   - Closing

3. **Speaker Notes / Email Body**
   - For each section: what to communicate and what tone to strike
   - Highlights: celebrate without bragging
   - Lowlights: be honest without being alarming
   - Asks: be specific and make it easy for investors to help

Investor updates should take investors 5 minutes to read and make them feel informed, confident, and useful. Never hide bad news — share it with a plan.
```

```
You are a strategy consultant who helps companies create annual strategy presentations. Create an annual strategy deck.

Industry: [INDUSTRY]

Current situation:
- Last year's performance: [SUMMARY — KEY METRICS AND ACHIEVEMENTS]
- Biggest wins last year: [LIST 3]

- Market conditions: [CURRENT STATE OF YOUR MARKET]

- 3-year vision: [WHERE YOU WANT TO BE IN 3 YEARS]
- This year's theme: [A UNIFYING THEME FOR THE YEAR — e.g., "The Year of the Customer," "Scale with Discipline"]
- Annual goals: [LIST 3-5 HIGH-LEVEL GOALS FOR THE YEAR]

Strategic priorities:
- Priority 1: [WHAT AND WHY]
- Priority 2: [WHAT AND WHY]
- Priority 3: [WHAT AND WHY]
- Priority 4: [WHAT AND WHY] (optional)

Resources:
- Budget: [$AMOUNT — and how it compares to last year]
- Team: [PLANNED HEADCOUNT AND KEY HIRES]
- Technology/infrastructure investments: [KEY INVESTMENTS]

Generate a 15-slide annual strategy deck:

1. **Opening Slide** — The year's theme: one powerful phrase + a visual description
2. **Year in Review** — Honest assessment: what we achieved, what we missed, key lessons

4. **Customer Insights** — What we learned about our customers this year
5. **3-Year Vision** — Where we're headed: the north star

9. **Strategic Priority 3** — Same format
10. **Strategic Priority 4** — Same format (or combine 3 and 4 if only 3 priorities)
11. **Financial Plan Summary** — Revenue targets, investment allocation, key financial milestones
12. **Organization & Team Plan** — How the team will evolve: hiring, structure, capabilities
13. **Annual OKRs / Goals** — Measurable goals with quarterly milestones
14. **Risk & Contingency** — Top risks and how we'll monitor/mitigate them
15. **Call to Action** — What every person in the room needs to focus on starting now

Speaker notes should inspire and align. A strategy deck isn't just a plan — it's a rallying cry. Make people feel like they understand where we're going and they're excited to contribute.

### Prompt 8 — Strategic Initiative Business Case

Use this to create a business case presentation for a specific strategic initiative that needs approval.

```
You are a strategy director creating a business case for a strategic initiative. Create a business case presentation.

Initiative name: [NAME OF THE INITIATIVE]
Proposed by: [YOUR NAME AND DEPARTMENT]
Audience: [WHO NEEDS TO APPROVE — e.g., "executive team," "board," "VP of Product"]

The opportunity:
- What: [DESCRIBE THE INITIATIVE IN 2-3 SENTENCES]
- Why: [STRATEGIC RATIONALE — WHY NOW]
- Impact: [EXPECTED BUSINESS IMPACT]

Current state: [HOW THINGS WORK TODAY]
Proposed future state: [HOW THINGS WOULD WORK AFTER THE INITIATIVE]
Investment required: [$AMOUNT — breakdown: people, technology, external costs]
Timeline: [EXPECTED DURATION — PHASES IF APPLICABLE]
Expected ROI: [RETURN AND TIMEFRAME]
Risks: [TOP 3 RISKS]
Alternatives considered: [OTHER OPTIONS YOU EVALUATED AND REJECTED]

Generate a 10-slide business case:

1. **Executive Summary** — The initiative in 30 seconds: what, why, how much, expected return
2. **Strategic Context** — How this initiative fits into the company's broader strategy
3. **Current State Analysis** — The problem or opportunity with data
4. **Proposed Solution** — What you want to do and how
5. **Implementation Plan** — Phased approach with milestones and timeline
6. **Financial Model** — Investment, expected returns, payback period, NPV if applicable
7. **Risk Assessment** — Risks with probability, impact, and mitigation
8. **Alternatives Analysis** — Options considered and why this one is recommended
9. **Team & Resources** — Who's needed and what they'll do
10. **Recommendation & Decision Needed** — Clear ask with decision criteria

Speaker notes should anticipate objections. For every slide, prepare for "why not wait?" and "what if this fails?" questions.
```

### Prompt 9 — SWOT / Strategic Analysis Presentation

Use this to present a structured strategic analysis of your company, market, or a specific opportunity.

```
You are a strategy analyst. Create a SWOT analysis presentation that goes beyond basic bullet points into actionable strategy.

Subject of analysis: [COMPANY / PRODUCT / MARKET / OPPORTUNITY]
Context: [WHY THIS ANALYSIS IS BEING DONE — e.g., "annual planning," "market entry decision," "competitive response"]
Audience: [WHO WILL SEE THIS]

Provide your inputs (I'll help structure and expand them):
Strengths: [LIST 3-5]
Weaknesses: [LIST 3-5]
Opportunities: [LIST 3-5]
Threats: [LIST 3-5]

Additional context:
- Market size/growth: [IF RELEVANT]
- Competitive position: [WHERE YOU RANK]
- Key resources: [WHAT YOU HAVE TO WORK WITH]
- Key constraints: [WHAT'S HOLDING YOU BACK]

Generate an 8-slide strategic analysis:

1. **Analysis Overview** — What we're analyzing and why, with the framework being used

2. **Strengths Slide** — Each strength with:
   - Why it's a genuine strength (evidence)
   - How to leverage it further
   - Risk of this strength becoming a weakness (complacency)

3. **Weaknesses Slide** — Each weakness with:
   - Honest assessment of severity (high/medium/low)
   - Root cause
   - Mitigation strategy and timeline

4. **Opportunities Slide** — Each opportunity with:
   - Size of the opportunity (quantified if possible)
   - Time sensitivity (is there a window?)
   - What's needed to capture it

5. **Threats Slide** — Each threat with:
   - Probability and potential impact
   - Early warning indicators to monitor
   - Defensive strategy

6. **SWOT Cross-Analysis Slide** — The most valuable part: how elements interact
   - Strength + Opportunity = Offensive strategies (how to win)
   - Strength + Threat = Defensive strategies (how to protect)
   - Weakness + Opportunity = Improvement strategies (what to fix to capture upside)
   - Weakness + Threat = Survival strategies (what to avoid or mitigate urgently)

7. **Strategic Recommendations** — 3-5 specific recommendations based on the analysis:
   - Recommendation | Rationale | Priority | Timeline | Owner

8. **Decision Framework** — What needs to be decided and by when

Speaker notes should connect every observation to a specific action. SWOT analyses are useless without "so what?" This presentation answers that question for every element.
```

## All-Hands & Company Meetings

### Prompt 10 — Company All-Hands Presentation

Use this for a company-wide meeting that updates everyone on business performance, celebrates wins, and aligns on what's next.

```
You are a CEO who runs outstanding all-hands meetings that employees actually look forward to. Create an all-hands presentation.

Company: [COMPANY NAME]
Company size: [NUMBER OF EMPLOYEES]
Frequency: [MONTHLY / QUARTERLY / AD-HOC]
Format: [IN-PERSON / VIRTUAL / HYBRID]
Duration: [30 MIN / 45 MIN / 60 MIN]
Current company mood: [e.g., "energized," "uncertain," "fatigued," "celebrating a win"]

Business update:
- Revenue/growth: [HIGH-LEVEL FIGURES — don't need to be exact for all employees]
- Key wins: [3-5 THINGS TO CELEBRATE]
- Challenges: [1-2 THINGS TO BE HONEST ABOUT]
- Product updates: [WHAT SHIPPED OR IS SHIPPING SOON]
- Customer wins: [NOTABLE CUSTOMER STORIES]
- Team updates: [NEW HIRES, DEPARTURES, PROMOTIONS, ORGANIZATIONAL CHANGES]

Strategic context:
- Where we're headed: [STRATEGIC DIRECTION IN PLAIN LANGUAGE]
- What's changing: [ANY CHANGES EMPLOYEES NEED TO KNOW ABOUT]
- What's not changing: [WHAT STAYS THE SAME — IMPORTANT FOR STABILITY]

Special topics: [ANY SPECIAL ANNOUNCEMENTS — e.g., funding, acquisition, office changes, new benefits]

Generate a 12-slide all-hands presentation:

1. **Opening** — Set the tone: energetic, honest, or both. A one-liner that captures the state of the company.

2. **Celebrating Wins** — 3-5 achievements with specific callouts:
   - What happened
   - Who made it happen (name names — recognition matters)
   - Why it matters for the company

3. **Customer Spotlight** — A customer story that makes everyone proud of the work they do

4. **Business Update** — Key metrics in simple terms:
   - Revenue trend (up/down/flat with context)
   - Customer growth
   - Market position
   - One metric that matters most right now

5. **Product Update** — What's new and what's coming:
   - Recent launches
   - Upcoming features to get excited about
   - Customer feedback highlights

6. **Honest Conversation** — What's challenging:
   - Be specific (don't say "market headwinds" — say what you mean)
   - What we're doing about it
   - What each person can do to help

7. **Team Updates** — Welcome new team members, announce promotions, acknowledge departures with respect

8. **Strategic Direction** — Where we're going in plain English:
   - The big picture: what does success look like?
   - The immediate focus: what matters this quarter?
   - How each department contributes

9. **Special Announcement(s)** — If applicable:
   - The announcement
   - Context and what it means for employees
   - FAQs

10. **Values in Action** — A specific example of company values being lived out (not theoretical — a real story)

11. **What's Next** — Top 3 company priorities with clear, simple language:
    - Priority 1: [WHAT] — [WHY IT MATTERS]
    - Priority 2
    - Priority 3

12. **Q&A / Open Forum** — How to ask questions (live, anonymous, Slack, etc.)

Speaker notes should balance transparency with confidence. Employees can handle truth — what they can't handle is being kept in the dark. Be honest about challenges while being clear about the plan.
```

### Prompt 11 — Change Management / Organizational Change Presentation

Use this when you need to communicate a significant organizational change to your team.

```
You are a change management expert. Create a presentation for communicating an organizational change.

Company: [COMPANY NAME]
The change: [DESCRIBE THE CHANGE — e.g., "reorganization," "layoffs," "new strategy," "office closure," "leadership change," "merger/acquisition"]
Why the change is happening: [ROOT CAUSE — be honest]
Who is affected: [WHICH TEAMS/ROLES/LOCATIONS]
Timeline: [WHEN THIS TAKES EFFECT]
Audience: [WHO YOU'RE PRESENTING TO — e.g., "all employees," "affected team," "managers first"]

What stays the same: [IMPORTANT ANCHORS OF STABILITY]
What changes: [SPECIFIC CHANGES]
Support available: [e.g., "severance packages," "retraining," "career coaching," "town halls for questions"]
Who people can talk to: [CONTACT PERSON OR PROCESS FOR QUESTIONS]

Generate an 8-slide change management presentation:

1. **Context Slide** — Why this change is happening:
   - Business context (market conditions, strategic direction, financial reality)
   - The decision-making process (show it was thoughtful, not reactive)
   - Tone: honest, empathetic, not corporate-speak

2. **The Change Slide** — What's changing, specifically:
   - Clear, unambiguous description
   - What it means for the audience
   - Timeline and key dates

3. **What's NOT Changing Slide** — Stability anchors:
   - What remains the same (mission, values, key relationships)
   - What people can count on
   - This slide exists because people need something to hold onto

4. **The Why Slide** — Deeper rationale:
   - How this change positions us for success
   - What the alternatives were and why this was chosen
   - The intended outcome

5. **What It Means for You Slide** — Personal impact:
   - Reporting changes
   - Role changes
   - Location changes
   - Process changes
   - Timeline for transitions

6. **Support & Resources Slide**
   - What support is available (specific programs, not vague promises)
   - Who to contact for questions
   - FAQ resources
   - Counseling or coaching if applicable

7. **What We Need from You Slide**
   - What leadership is asking of the team
   - How people can contribute to a smooth transition
   - Patience and grace expectations

8. **Next Steps & Communication Plan**
   - Immediate next steps (this week, this month)
   - Future communication schedule (when the next update comes)
   - How to ask questions (anonymously if needed)
   - Commitment: "We will be transparent throughout this process"

Speaker notes should be deeply empathetic. Acknowledge that change is hard. Don't rush through bad news. Pause after the difficult slides. Show that leadership understands the human impact.

### Prompt 14 — Post-Mortem / Retrospective Presentation

Use this to create a post-mortem or retrospective presentation after a project, initiative, or incident.

```
You are a post-mortem facilitator. Create a retrospective presentation that drives learning, not blame.

Subject: [WHAT THE POST-MORTEM IS ABOUT — e.g., "Q3 product launch," "outage on March 15," "failed marketing campaign"]
Outcome: [WAS IT A SUCCESS, PARTIAL SUCCESS, OR FAILURE?]
Timeline: [WHEN IT HAPPENED AND HOW LONG IT LASTED]
Audience: [WHO WILL ATTEND THE RETROSPECTIVE]
Team involved: [WHO WORKED ON THIS]

What happened:
- [KEY EVENT 1 WITH DATE]
- [KEY EVENT 2]
- [KEY EVENT 3]
- [KEY EVENT 4]

What went well: [LIST 3-5 POSITIVES]
What went wrong: [LIST 3-5 ISSUES]
Root causes identified: [UNDERLYING CAUSES, NOT SURFACE SYMPTOMS]
Impact: [BUSINESS IMPACT — QUANTIFIED WHERE POSSIBLE]
Actions already taken: [WHAT'S BEEN DONE SINCE]

Generate an 8-slide retrospective:

1. **Context Slide** — What happened, in factual terms:
   - Timeline of events
   - Who was involved
   - The objective vs. actual outcome

2. **What Went Well** — Celebrate what worked:
   - Specific positives with evidence
   - What we should keep doing
   - Who deserves recognition

3. **What Didn't Go Well** — Honest assessment:
   - Specific issues (not vague complaints)
   - Impact of each issue
   - Framed as process/system failures, not personal failures

4. **Timeline / Incident Analysis** — Detailed chronological view:
   - Events mapped on a timeline
   - Decision points marked
   - Where things went right vs. wrong

5. **Root Cause Analysis**
   - For each major issue: the "5 Whys" analysis
   - System/process factors (not individual blame)
   - Contributing factors vs. root causes

6. **Impact Assessment**
   - Business impact quantified (revenue, customers, time, reputation)
   - Internal impact (team morale, confidence, technical debt)
   - Positive impacts (if any — sometimes failures teach valuable lessons)

7. **Action Items**
   - Specific actions with:
     - Description
     - Owner
     - Due date
     - Expected outcome
     - Priority (high/medium/low)

8. **Lessons Learned Summary**
   - 3-5 key lessons in quotable format
   - How these lessons change our approach going forward
   - Commitment to follow through on action items

Speaker notes should model a blame-free culture. The facilitator's job is to focus on systems and processes, not individuals. Every "who" question should be redirected to a "what" or "how" question.
```

## Data Storytelling Presentations

### Prompt 15 — Data-Driven Presentation Builder

Use this when you have data and need to turn it into a compelling narrative presentation.

```
You are a data storytelling expert. Help me turn raw data into a compelling presentation narrative.

Data I have:
- [DATASET 1: DESCRIBE — e.g., "12 months of website traffic data showing 3x growth"]
- [DATASET 2: DESCRIBE — e.g., "customer survey results from 500 respondents"]
- [DATASET 3: DESCRIBE — e.g., "revenue by product line, quarterly for 2 years"]
- [DATASET 4: DESCRIBE — e.g., "competitor market share data"]

The story I want to tell: [WHAT CONCLUSION OR RECOMMENDATION DOES THIS DATA SUPPORT?]
Audience: [WHO — AND THEIR DATA LITERACY LEVEL]
Presentation length: [HOW MANY SLIDES / MINUTES]
Decision I want to drive: [WHAT SHOULD THE AUDIENCE DO WITH THIS INFORMATION?]

Generate:

1. **Data Narrative Framework** — The story structure:
   - Setup: the question or hypothesis we started with
   - Discovery: what the data reveals (build suspense)
   - Insight: the "aha" moment — the non-obvious conclusion
   - Implication: what this means for the audience
   - Action: what to do about it

2. **Slide-by-Slide Plan** — For each slide:
   - What data to show
   - Chart/visualization type recommendation (bar, line, scatter, pie, table, etc.)
   - The headline that tells the insight (not just describes the chart)
   - Supporting text (one sentence)
   - What to say (speaker notes — interpreting the data, not reading the chart)

3. **Chart Headline Best Practices**
   - BAD: "Revenue by Quarter" (describes the chart)
   - GOOD: "Revenue Doubled in Q3 After Launching Premium Tier" (tells the insight)
   - Generate headlines for each of my data slides using this principle

4. **Data Simplification** — For each complex dataset:
   - What to include on the slide (the essential)
   - What to move to the appendix (the detail)
   - How to annotate charts for clarity

5. **Transitions Between Data Points** — Connecting language:
   - How to move from one data slide to the next
   - How to build a cumulative argument
   - Where to pause for audience processing

6. **The "So What?" Test** — For each data point:
   - The data says: [FACT]
   - This means: [INTERPRETATION]
   - So we should: [ACTION]

Help me present data that drives decisions, not data that puts people to sleep.
```
